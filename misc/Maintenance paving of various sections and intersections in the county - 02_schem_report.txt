1.1 Introduction
The Westport Corridor and Community Plan represents proposed improvements for the Westport
Community that have been arrived at through an exchange of ideas between local citizens, business
owners, county representatives, technical advisers and input from various public agencies. It is
comprised of the following five project components:
     New Access off Highway 30 to Westport Ferry/Boat Ramp
     Pedestrian Safety and Aesthetic Improvements to Highway 30
     Plympton Creek Historic Channel Reconnection between Ferry Landing and Boat Ramp
     Georgia Pacific Voluntary Clean-Up Program and Transition to County Park with Transit Stop
     Boat Landing Reconstruction and Improvements

The Schematic Design was broken down into two separate phases: Phase 1 involved a feasibility
analysis of all five projects. This feasibility analysis assessed the permitability, level of community
support and availability of funding for each component. Please see Lower Columbia Engineering
report titled “Westport Corridor and Community Plan, Phase 1 - Feasibility Analysis Report” dated
January 7, 2011. Based on the findings of Phase 1, the team proceeded with schematic design of all of
the projects in Phase 2. Schematic design documents were developed in Phase 2 to provide the
information necessary to help secure funding and move forward with the next steps towards
implementation of each of the projects.

1.2 Phase 2 Summary
The objective of Phase 2 is to develop schematic design drawings of each of the five projects and to
provide research and supporting data that help these projects become candidates for funding and
implementation. During Phase 1 (Feasibility Analysis), the projects were evaluated from several
different perspectives. The major factors involved with analyzing the feasibility of each project
included:
     Planning, Zoning and Land Use Regulations
     Transportation Traffic Analysis and Pedestrian Safety
     Westport Community and Business Support
     Public Transportation Connectivity between Clatsop and Columbia Counties
     Availability of Grants and other Funding Sources
     Environmental Assessment, Cleanup and Mitigation

In Phase 2 additional research data and schematic plans were developed based on the findings of Phase
1. Sections 2.1 through 2.5 briefly summarize the schematic designs for each of the five projects. The
initial concerns and challenges for each project are explained at the beginning of each project section.
Following this summary, there are schematic design plans for each project. Reference material
supporting the schematic designs is included in Section 2.6, Section 2.7 and Appendices 1 and 2.
Section 2.6 is a report from DKS Associates titled “Westport Community Plan Transportation Impact
Study”. Section 2.7 is a report from DKS Associates titled “Westport US 30 Pedestrian and Safety
Enhancement”. There is additional reference information provided in Appendix 1 and Appendix 2
which includes, Contact log, meeting notes, notices and technical reports. Although each of the five
projects has planning, design and funding challenges, all five projects were supported by the
community and various applicable agencies.

Section 2.1                                                        New Access off Highway 30


Existing Concerns
The existing Westport Ferry Road is squeezed between Plympton Creek and several residential properties.
This current configuration poses the following primary issues of concern:
       • Existing roadway does not meet standards for width or construction type
       • Inadequate flood plain for Plympton Creek
       • Flooding and Erosion of road base during high flow events
       • Public Safety and disruption to residential neighborhood with no provisions for pedestrian or
           bicycle use. This issue has the potential to become a larger problem than it already is based on
           plans to increase ferry capacity and develop industrial sites.

Summary of Findings and Recommendations
Major public roadway improvements connecting Highway 30 to the Ferry Landing are clearly a
necessity and have good support from the community and adjacent land owners. Although there
were differences of opinion expressed at the community meetings about the best way to approach
this issue, the community is supportive of a reconnection of Westport Ferry Road to a new access
off Highway 30. In Section 2.6 - Westport Community Plan Transportation Impact Study, page 1,
DKS Associates explains the need for the New Access Road to Highway 30.

“Potential growth and infrastructure improvements in Westport that create the need for this
evaluation include increased capacity at the ferry along the Westport Slough of the Columbia River,
improvements to the existing recreational boat ramp, and potential activity at the heavy industrial
area west of the ferry terminal. This study also evaluates the feasibility of a new collector roadway
connecting the highway with the ferry and adjacent improvements. This road would be located west
of the community and would require a new highway access and rail crossing.
This transportation evaluation considers safety, livability, and operations resulting from three
potential growth, development, and improvement scenarios. Particular emphasis is placed on
providing adequate access to US 30. Figure 1 (in the DKS report) shows the study area, including
the potential future development and roadway facilities being considered. The three study
intersections for focused evaluation are shown in Figure 1 (in the DKS report) and include the
following:
   •   US 30/Old Mill Town Road
   •   US 30/Westport Ferry Road
   •   US 30/Old Highway 30

The evaluation identified that without the ability to widen Westport Ferry Road to provide
sidewalks, bike lanes, and adequate pavement, in addition to not being able to provide turn lanes
from US 30, the current roadway would not be considered adequate to serve future traffic based on
community livability and safety criteria. Therefore, a new collector roadway connection US 30 to
the ferry terminal is the preferred alternative.”

Transportation Plan Recommendations
See section 2.6 – Westport Community Plan Transportation Impact Study for a complete report by
DKS Associates. Pages 38 and 39 of the report give a summary of plan recommendations for the
New Access to Highway 30.

“ While the new collector road would require a new highway access and an at-grade rail crossing, it
would improve community livability by removing truck traffic from Westport Ferry Road. In
addition, it would accommodate turn lanes on US 30, which would improve safety by removing
turning traffic from the through lanes. Therefore, this study recommends the following
improvements to implement the Westport Community Plan and support development potential in the
area:
   •   Construct a new collector roadway between US 30 and Westport Ferry Road to ensure that
       additional development and increased ferry and heavy industrial traffic in Westport does not
       degrade community livability.
   •   Close the existing at-grade rail crossing of Westport Ferry Road. Consider providing an
       emergency vehicle only crossing at this location.
   •   Construct westbound and eastbound left-turn lanes on US 30 at the new collector roadway
       (i.e., which will access US 30 across from the existing Old Highway 30 approach). In
       addition, construct a westbound right-turn lane at this intersection. During the design process,
       consideration should be given for extending the eastbound right-turn and westbound left-turn
       lanes to serve potential queue overflow of a worst-case train blockage scenario.
   •   Amend the Clatsop County Transportation System Plan to show the proposed collector
       roadway.
   •   After the Clatsop County TSP is amended, apply for a grant of access with ODOT for the
       proposed access to US 30. If the grant of access is approved, the County should purchase
       access rights based on an ODOT assessment of the increased value of the properties served
       by the proposed access location.
These improvements are consistent with the various long-term transportation plans pertaining to the
area, including providing adequate mobility on US 30, providing adequate access to the ferry,
serving future development areas, and protecting community livability. The proposed collector
roadway, which would amend the County's TSP, would meet applicable ODOT operations and
safety requirements (i.e., mobility standards and turn-lane criteria) and therefore, OAR 660-012-
0060 requirements would be met.”
The closing of the existing at-grade rail crossing of Westport Ferry Road is a concern for some folks
within the Westport community. There are ongoing discussions in progress with ODOT Rail to
pursue possible alternatives to closing this rail crossing. DKS received feedback from ODOT Rail on
May 27, 2011 regarding their current policy. The state adopted ORS 824, Sections 202,204 and 206
that gives authority to ODOT Rail for regulating the rail system and rail crossings. This statute also
includes direction to eliminate at-grade crossings wherever possible. To implement the legislative
direction to eliminate at-grade crossings wherever possible, ODOT Rail has a policy to not allow


                                                                                                      7
new rail crossings (which they normally only allow exceptions to under overwhelming
circumstances in support of the new crossing for multiple benefits).
For the new collector to be built with an at-grade crossing (which is not ODOT Rail’s preference), it
would have to be treated as a re-alignment of an existing roadway to satisfy the no new at-grade
crossing policy. The applicant for the new at-grade crossing would be Clatsop County. If the County
files the permit order request and in that, object to closing the existing Westport Ferry Road
crossing, then there is potential for a hearing (with a hearings officer) to legally debate the issue. The
county would need to lead the charge to do something different in Westport, and they would need to
be prepared (with very compelling arguments) to lead the charge in a hearing process.
  
Planning, Zoning and Land Use Findings for New Ferry Access Road to Highway 30
The findings noted in the Wingard Planning and Development Report, See Appendix 2 – Reference

recommendations of the Transportation Report.
Page 4 and 5 in the report state:
“The new ferry access road is not specified as a project in the 2003 Clatsop County Transportation
System Plan (TSP). As a result, construction of the new roadway may not be possible unless it were
completed as an emergency measure or as an accessory development to another permitted principal
use in the zone. Teevin's industrial operation or the ferry landing itself would qualify as
(conditionally) permitted principal uses in the HI zone but assigning an “accessory use” label to the
new public road that would serve these developments, plus several other land uses in the area, would
likely bog down the various land use, transportation and environmental permitting processes.

The solution to this potential problem is to amend the Clatsop County TSP to include the new ferry
access road as a specific project. An amendment to the County's TSP is considered an amendment
to the Goal 12 (Transportation) element of the Clatsop County Comprehensive Plan. Public hearings
are required before the County Planning Commission and Board of Commissioners. A three to six
month processing time should be anticipated once a complete plan amendment application is
remitted to the County Planning Division.

A review of the TSP finds several excerpts that lay a solid foundation for refining the county's
transportation plan to ultimately include many of the Westport Corridor and Community Plan project
components.”

Existing Concerns

       • Vehicles tend to speed through town
       • The Highway bisects the town with no crosswalks, adequate pedestrian walkways or bicycle

Summary of Findings and Recommendations
The implementation of Pedestrian Improvements to Highway 30 has broad support from the
community and ODOT. Most comments from those at the community meetings were very positive
about the safety improvements proposed to Highway 30. In Section 2.7 - Westport US 30
Pedestrian and Safety Enhancement Concepts, page 1, DKS Associates explains the need for the
pedestrian safety improvements.

“Within the community of Westport, US 30 bisects neighborhoods and commercial developments,
which creates the potential for pedestrian crossings on the uncontrolled approaches of US 30. To
improve driver and pedestrian awareness, as well as multimodal safety (bicycle, pedestrian and
motor vehicle), several improvements are recommended for two intersections and along the US 30
roadway segment within the community.”

slower speeds through the Westport community. The recommendations are focusing on balancing

    • Realign intersections of US 30/Old Mill Town Road and US 30/Westport Ferry Road to be

    • Construct bulb-outs at intersections
    • Improve illumination at intersection crossing locations
    • Provide pedestrian signing near crossing locations

    Section 2.3                                    Plympton Creek Restoration to Original Bed
 
 
    Existing Concerns
    The lower reach of Plympton Creek was likely re-routed at some point to accommodate development.
    Following is a list of concerns with the current configuration:
           • In High flow events, Plympton Creek jumps the bank and floods the Ferry Landing Road just
              before the bridge
           • A large amount of sediment is discharged at the Ferry Landing based on the current confluence
              with the Westport Slough
           • Essential tidal habitat is lacking in this portion of the creek
           • The current configuration does not provide the maximum benefit to the community
 
    Summary of Findings and Recommendations
    The restoration of Plympton Creek to its original bed has the support of the Lower Columbia
    Watershed Council, Columbia Soil and Water Conservation District (CSWCD), Clatsop County
    and Landowner Georgia Pacific (GP), pending finalization of their clean-up agreement with DEQ.
    The concept was also generally well received by the Westport community as determined by
    interviews with Westport citizens and community meetings.
 
    Initially, the goal for the Plympton Creek project was to produce schematic design documents that
    would be submitted to OWEB for possible grant funds by April 16, 2011. However, GP is
    currently focused on working with the DEQ under the Voluntary Clean-up Program to close the
    site, which is hoped to be completed by December, 2012. As a result, it was recommended by Bill
    Bennett from Lower Columbia Estuary Partnership that the project move forward with technical
    assistance grants that will allow the design process to continue. This will allow the design team
    time to work with GP on the final location of Plympton Creek, as well as allow GP time to
    coordinate and evaluate the relocation impacts to the clean up.
 
    Planning, Zoning and Land Use Findings

    “A zone change (comprehensive plan/zoning map amendment) from Heavy Industrial (HI) to Lake
    and Wetlands (LW) is needed to facilitate the Plympton Creek restoration project.”

Section 2.5                                 Boat Landing Reconstruction and Improvement

Existing Concerns
The Boat Landing reconstruction and improvement is considered a high priority for the State Marine Board
for the following reasons:
         This is a popular recreation area, particularly for spring salmon fishing. During spring salmon
            season, it is not uncommon to have trucks/trailers lining both sides of the access road back
            around the corner on Old Mill Town Road.
         The current ramp gets high use and is in terrible shape. The next improved public ramp
            accessing the Columbia River to the West is in John Day 22 miles away.
         There are currently no: Public Restrooms (only a porta-potty), lighting or reasonable security
            measures.

Project Challenges
        Resistance to improvement as this will increase usage by non-residents.
        Finding the balance between the goals of the County, Community of Westport & State Marine
          Board.

Summary of Findings and Recommendations
As noted in the Westport Corridor and Community Plan Phase 1 Feasibility Analysis dated January
7, 2011, this is a high priority project for the State Marine Board. Lower Columbia Engineering
developed a schematic design based on a preliminary drawing provided by the State Marine Board.
The community support for the Boat Ramp improvements was very positive. Several suggestions
were made by the community as a result of three public meetings at the Westport Community
Church and other meetings with community members. The community agreed that a park host was a
good idea and necessary for safety and to prevent vandalism. The community felt that the park host
would benefit both the Boat Landing as well as the proposed future county park. An RV site for a
full time host was shown on the schematic design drawing. Tyler Joki from the Columbia Soil and
Water Conservation district suggested that a boat wash for invasive aquatic species be incorporated
into the Boat Landing schematic plan. This is becoming a priority through funding by the State
Marine program to combat invasive species. Most community members felt that the parking shown
on the schematic design drawing was adequate for the boat landing, even during the busy periods of
spring fishing season (approximately 75 parking locations). Another issue that was important to the
local community was appropriate security lighting. These important concerns should be incorporated
into the final design. The need for improvements to the existing boat ramp was clearly supported by
the Westport community. The improvements to the existing ramp should be coordinated with the
Oregon State Marine Board.

At the community meetings, there were some residents who were concerned about increased traffic
on Old Mill Town Road as a result of the improvements to the Boat Landing (even with the
anticipated development of the New Access Road off Highway 30). However, other community
members felt that adequate signage on Highway 30 would direct most of the traffic to the New
Access Road as opposed to Old Mill Town Road. This issue should be reviewed further as the
projects continue to develop.

CHAPTER 1: EXECUTIVE SUMMARY
This study evaluates future transportation improvements proposed in Westport, Oregon in 
association with potential growth and planned infrastructure improvements. Westport is located in 
Clatsop County between Astoria and Clatskanie on US 30, adjacent to the Columbia River. This study 
evaluates future year scenarios to determine appropriate improvements, including satisfying 
Transportation Planning Rule requirements (OAR 660‐012‐0060) for improvements that would 
impact the surrounding network and/or amend the County's Transportation System Plan.  
Potential growth and infrastructure improvements in Westport that create the need for this 
evaluation include increased capacity at the ferry along the Westport Slough of the Columbia River, 
improvements to the existing recreational boat ramp, and potential activity at the heavy industrial 
area west of the ferry terminal. This study also evaluates the feasibility of a new collector roadway 
connecting the highway with the ferry and adjacent improvements. This road would be located west 
of the community and would require a new highway access and rail crossing. 
This transportation evaluation considers safety, livability, and operations resulting from three 
potential growth, development, and improvement scenarios. Particular emphasis is placed on 
providing adequate access to US 30. Figure 1 shows the study area, including the potential future 
development and roadway facilities being considered. The three study intersections for focused 
evaluation are shown in Figure 1 and include the following: 
        US 30/Old Mill Town Road 
        US 30/Westport Ferry Road 
        US 30/Old Highway 30 

The evaluation identified that without the ability to widen Westport Ferry Road to provide 
sidewalks, bike lanes, and adequate pavement, in addition to not being able to provide turn lanes 
from US 30, the current roadway would not be considered adequate to serve future traffic based on 
community livability and safety criteria.  Therefore, a new collector roadway connection US 30 to 
the ferry terminal is the preferred alternative. 
While the new collector road would require a new highway access and an at‐grade rail crossing, it 
would improve community livability by removing truck traffic from Westport Ferry Road. In 
addition, it would accommodate turn lanes on US 30, which would improve safety by removing 
turning traffic from the through lanes. Therefore, this study recommends the following 
improvements to implement the Westport Community Plan and support development potential in 
the area: 
        Construct a new collector roadway between US 30 and Westport Ferry Road to ensure that 
         additional development and increased ferry and heavy industrial traffic in Westport does not 
         degrade community livability. 
        Close the existing at‐grade rail crossing of Westport Ferry Road.  Consider providing an 
         emergency vehicle only crossing at this location.

Oregon Highway Plan
The Oregon Highway Plan (OHP)1 applies the general directives developed as part of the 1992 
Oregon Transportation Plan to the state highway system. The OHP presents goals, policies, actions, 
system elements, and standards to direct planning efforts for the state’s highway system. 
Specifically, it provides highway classifications, mobility standards, and access management 
standards applicable to US 30. 

US 30 Classification
The OHP classifies US 30 as a Statewide Highway, Freight Route, and Truck Route on the National 
Highway System. Excluding interstates, this is the highest classification for a highway within the 
State of Oregon. Maintaining mobility and operational efficiency for the movement of goods and 
people is an important goal for ODOT on these types of facilities. These types of facilities provide 
inter‐urban and inter‐regional mobility with connections to larger urban area, ports, and major 
recreation areas. “The management objective is to provide safe and efficient, high‐speed, 
continuous‐flow operation. In constrained and urban areas, interruptions to flow should be 
minimal.”2 

Mobility Standards
The OHP identifies applicable intersection mobility standards along State highways, such as US 30 
through Westport. The mobility standards are given as volume to capacity (v/c) ratios and apply to 
30th highest hourly traffic conditions.3 Because this section of US 30 is a Freight Route on a 
Statewide Highway and is outside an urban growth boundary (UGB), the applicable mobility 
standard for all three study intersections is a v/c ratio of 0.70.

Access Management Standards
The OHP also identifies access spacing standards, which are given as the measurement of the 
approach road spacing from center to center on the same side of the roadway. For US 30 (which is a 
Statewide Highway) the applicable spacing standards are 1,320 feet when the posted speed is 55 
miles per hour (mph) and 990 feet when the posted speed is 40 mph.4 

Highway Design Manual
The Highway Design Manual (HDM) has been developed by ODOT as the source for standards used 
in the development of improvements to Oregon’s highways.5 The authors of the manual have 
striven to be “in general agreement” with the American Association of State Transportation 
Officials’ (AASHTO) A Policy on the Geometric Design of Highways and Streets (2001). Standards 
provided in the HDM include roadway design standards and operating standards. Additional design 
guidance is also provided. 

Design Standards
US 30 is classified as a non‐freeway rural major arterial in the HDM. The design requirements for US 
30 are based on the number of lanes and Average Daily Traffic (ADT).6 The current and future 
Average Annual Daily Traffic (AADT) as reported by ODOT for US 30 in Westport are 7,600 (2009) 
and 10,600 (2029), respectively. Table 1 shows the applicable design standards for US 30 in 
Westport.

Operating Standards
The HDM also specifies intersection operating standards that new capacity improvement projects 
being designed and built on State highways should meet to ensure a 20‐year design life.7 Because

Clatsop County TSP
The Clatsop County Transportation System Plan (TSP)8 was prepared in 2003 and documents 
transportation facilities, needs, standards, and improvement projects. As an unincorporated 
community within the County, Westport was included in the inventories and analysis. The 
information provided in the TSP can be categorized as applying generally to Westport, to US 30, and 
to Westport Ferry Road. 

General Westport Needs
As stated in the County TSP, Westport is primarily a residential community with some commercial 
use along the highway and some heavy industrial land along the Columbia River. The flow of traffic 
to the industrial area has been highlighted as a problem by the County. Westport Ferry Road 
provides access for the community to the Westport Ferry, Columbia River, and industrial area. 
However, it “is a narrow residential road that is inundated with heavy industrial traffic.”9 The TSP 
indicates that the solution is to develop options that will better serve both the industrial area and 
residents who live along this stretch of the river. 
The TSP also indicates that the transportation facilities within the Westport (including US 30) are 
expected to support the community should only minor increases in density and development occur. 
In addition, the TSP indicates that the rail crossings should be improved if any safety issues are 
identified (this applies to all rail crossings west of Clatskanie). 

US 30
US 30 is a State Highway and is the primary roadway through Westport. It is also a bicycle route 
managed by the State. The County TSP identifies US 30 through Westport as a roadway where 
access management should be investigated. In addition, the TSP included operations analysis for the 
US 30/Westport Ferry Road intersection and identified future capacity needs in the year 2022 (i.e., 
the TSP’s future horizon year). Therefore, a future project (TSP Project 29) was identified to 
construct turn lanes and other improvements at this intersection. 

Westport Ferry Road
Westport Ferry Road is identified by the TSP as a County collector roadway. The County Roadway

feasible, reconstruction of existing roadway facilities. In addition, Collector roadway cross‐section 
standards indicate that bikeways are required on all collector roadways with six‐foot wide striped 
and stenciled bike lanes, except where special constraints exist or where exempted by County

Portland-Astoria (US 30) Corridor Plan
The Portland‐Astoria (US 30) Corridor Plan10 was completed by ODOT Regions 1 and 2 through a 
cooperative process with local and regional governments, agencies, and various stakeholders. It was 
adopted by the Oregon Transportation Commission in 1999 as an element of the Oregon 
Transportation Plan. The purpose of this plan was to develop a long term plan for management and 
improvements to the US 30 Corridor. The plan focuses on managing growth on the existing corridor 
through focused improvements to the existing facilities, with emphasis given to alternate modes. 
This plan mentions the Cathlamet‐Westport Ferry as one of the three connections between US 30 
and the Washington State highway system (the other two connections are at Rainier and Astoria). 
The Westport Toll Ferry links US 30 with Puget Island, which is connected to Washington State by 
Highway 409 and State Route 4, providing access to Cathlamet, Long Beach and Longview/Kelso. The 
corridor plan objective A.51 for waterborne transportation indicates that ferry service should be 
maintained between Cathlamet and Westport. 
While this corridor plan does not identify any specific projects within the community of Westport, 
there are several goals and policies that align well with the needs and concerns expressed by the 
community.11 Specifically, these goals and policies focus on improving user safety and highway 
operations for all modes through cost effective maintenance and spot improvements. For Rural 
Community Centers, three management directives have been identified for consistent corridor 
improvements: 
            Access Management 
            Intersection improvements to enhance access to the community center 
            Transportation improvements to support the health of economic community centers

            Recreational boat ramp, parking area, and county park improvements 
            Plympton Creek restoration to the original creek bed 
            New ferry access road construction 
            Pedestrian enhancements on US 30 
            Potential transit stop relocation

The development‐oriented projects will impact travel patterns and traffic operations on US 30 and 
at the key intersections of Old Mill Town road/US 30 and Westport Ferry Road/US 30. In particular, 
the ferry, boat ramp, and a potential for increased heavy industrial area activity will contribute to 
increased heavy vehicle traffic in Westport. 

Ferry Terminal and Service Improvements
Improvements have been identified for the Cathlamet‐Westport ferry boat and terminal to allow 
the ferry to transport semi‐trucks with trailers. The ferry boat’s current capacity is 12 passenger 
vehicles. With the improvements, the ferry boat would be able to transport up to two semi‐trucks 
with trailers. In addition to two semi‐trucks, the ferry boat would also be able to transport up to 18 
passenger vehicles. If only one semi‐truck is on the ferry, then it would be able to transport up to 21 
passenger vehicles. If no semi‐trucks are on the ferry, then it would be able to transport up to 24 
passenger vehicles. 

Boat Ramp, Parking Area, and County Park Improvements
The Westport boat landing is located off of Westport Ferry Road one‐half mile north of US 30. The 
boat landing provides access to the Westport Slough, which serves as a tributary to the Columbia 
River. The State Marine Board has proposed enhancements12 to the Westport boat landing, and 
Clatsop County intends to develop the surrounding property as a county park. The boat 
landing/county park site is located on a 27‐acre tax lot that is zoned for Lakes and Wetlands (LW) 
and Heavy Industrial (HI) land uses. The enhancements would be located on an approximately 15‐
acre portion of the site and would include an expansion of the parking lot, nature trails, and user 
amenities such as restrooms, and improved river access (boat ramp and fishing platforms). 
With the proposed enhancements, the boat landing/county park site would continue to serve its 
current users (e.g., salmon season fisherman) and would likely attract increases in both local and 
regional users. The majority of daily users would travel to the site by motor vehicle with a potential 
for a few daily walking trips from Westport. The traffic demand for the site would change 
throughout the year based on seasonal weather and fishing demand. 
The boat landing/county park site is accessed from US 30 using Old Mill Town Road and Westport 
Ferry Road. These roads are currently substandard and could require improvements to safely 
accommodate traffic demand from the proposed enhancements to the boat landing/county park 
site. 

Plympton Creek Restoration
Plympton Creek currently empties into the Westport Slough near the ferry terminal. As part of the 
ferry landing improvements, it would be restored to its original creek bed alignment to empty into 
the slough further to the east.

New Ferry Access Road
A new collector roadway is being considered on the west side of Westport connecting US 30 with 
Westport Ferry Road. The new roadway would provide alternative access to the Cathlamet‐
Westport ferry terminal, the Westport boat landing/county park site, and heavy industrial uses. The 
new roadway would be the designated truck route and would reduce the volume of non‐local traffic 
on Old Mill Town Road and Westport Ferry Road near the community center. 
To construct the new collector road, there are two important issues that must be addressed: 
            At‐grade rail crossing 
            US 30 access 

At-Grade Rail Crossing
The potential new roadway would require a crossing of the Burlington Northern Railroad. Due to 
cost and geometric constraints (e.g., the distance of the rail from US 30), an at‐grade rail crossing is 
desired. ODOT Rail has jurisdiction over rail crossing and would need to approve a new at‐grade 
crossing. ODOT Rail13 has provided the following feedback regarding the feasibility of a new crossing: 
            ODOT Rail has a zero new at‐grade rail crossings policy 
            The County should consider upgrading the current roads through Westport to serve the 
             need created by upgrading the ferry service, developing industrial lands, and improving the 
             recreational area 
            If the new road is determined to be the only acceptable option, it should be grade separated 
             over the rail 
            If the County insists on an at‐grade crossing for the new road, at a minimum one of the 
             current crossings in Westport would need to close so the new crossing could be considered 
             a re‐alignment of an existing road 

Due to the close spacing between US 30 and the railroad tracks (i.e., 350 feet), a grade‐separated 
railroad crossing may also require a grade‐separated interchange intersection with US 30 to allow 
for the potential new roadway to be high enough above the railroad to meet minimum clearance 
requirements (approximately 30' above the railroad). 

US 30 Access
According to access rights research completed by ODOT’s Right of Way Research Unit in Salem, 
access is completely restricted to US 30 from the subject property north of the highway (8N‐6W‐35‐
100 & 8N‐6W‐36‐409) through which access is desired. Therefore, a grant of access must be 
obtained from ODOT to reestablish a right of access and allow for the construction of a new 
approach. Because the proposed roadway would be a public approach, an access permit would not 
be required to operate the intersection after a grant of access is obtained.

The application procedure for a grant of access is described in OAR 734‐051‐0305, with the approval 
criteria described in OAR 734‐051‐0295. The process for review and approval of a grant of access, in 
addition to the following property appraisals and transactions, often takes several months to a year 
to complete. Furthermore, supportive information may be required for processing the grant of 
access application, such as a Traffic Impact Study addressing safety and long‐term compatibility with 
the existing and planned transportation system (some of which is addressed in this evaluation).  
Key criteria for gaining approval of a grant of access include: 
    1. Demonstrating that a grant of access is consistent with the 1999 Oregon Highway Plan, the 
       adopted Portland‐Astoria Corridor Plan for US 30 (November 1999), and a local 
       transportation system plan;  
                                                AND 
    2. A determination that ODOT no longer needs access control at the subject location in 
       consideration of the highway classification and designations, access spacing standards, 
       highway mobility standards, state and local transportation system plans, comprehensive 
       plan and land uses, and safety; 
                                                 OR 
    3. A determination that the grant of access will benefit the state highway system as defined in 
       OAR 734‐051‐0085 and OAR 734‐051‐0295(6)(e)(B)(i) through (iii).  

The Portland‐Astoria Corridor Plan for US 30 does not consider the proposed new approach to the 
highway, but does include maintaining ferry service between Cathlamet and Westport as an 
objective for waterborne transportation. Including the proposed approach in a local transportation 
system plan may be the most effective means of gaining approval for a grant of access as it, along 
with the supporting analysis/documentation, could be used as support multiple approval criteria. 
Therefore, we believe obtaining a grant of access for this roadway would require the following 
steps: 
        Perform a traffic study that would both satisfy Transportation Planning Rule requirements 
         for modifying the County's TSP to show the proposed road as a future collector street and 
         demonstrate the benefits of the roadway to satisfy the grant of access requirements. 
        Amend the Clatsop County Transportation System Plan and submit an application for a grant 
         of access. 
        If the grant of access is approved, the County would purchase access rights based on an 
         ODOT assessment of the increased value of the properties served by the proposed access 
         location.

Of particular interest is the US 30/Old Mill Town Road intersection. Within the community of 
Westport, US 30 bisects neighborhoods and commercial developments located north of the highway

Potential Transit Stop Relocation
In addition to these improvement options for pedestrian crossing safety, the location of the transit 
stop (transfer point between Columbia and Clatsop County service providers) should be closely 
coordinated with the location of pedestrian crossing improvements. If the transit stop continues to 
be located on US 30 near Old Mill Town Road, pedestrian crossing treatments should be focused at 
this location. If the transit stop location is moved to be co‐located with the Church parking lot, safe 
crossing are still needed, but a priority improvement would become providing adequate pedestrian 
facilities on the south side of US 30 to connect to the transit stop location. This could be 
accomplished with sidewalk or off‐street pathway improvements. 
If the transit transfer location is moved to be co‐located with the Ferry Terminal, pedestrian safety 
between the developed portion of Westport and the Ferry Terminal should be evaluated to provide 
a convenient walking connection. 

Potential Increase in Heavy Industrial Activity
Currently, there is heavy industrial use on lands west of the ferry terminal between Burlington 
Northern Railroad and the Westport Slough. In the past, this area has generated activity such as 
freight movement of aggregate material, which caused community concern for livability along 
Westport Ferry Road due to heavy vehicle traffic adjacent to residential properties. If the access to 
the ferry terminal is improved, there is potential for renewed heavy industrial activity and increased 
freight traffic.

Transportation Facilities Inventory
A transportation facilities inventory was performed for Westport and included the roadway 
characteristics, cross‐sections, and railroad crossings. The key study area roadways are first 
described followed by Table 2, which lists roadway characteristics and cross‐sections, and Table 3, 
which lists the railroad crossing characteristics. 
US 30 is the primary roadway through Westport and has a 40 mile per hour (mph) speed limit 
through town and a 55 mph speed limit outside of town. The highway is a marked two lane facility 
with areas of on‐street parking adjacent to commercial activities and a gravel shoulder for other 
segments. Sight distance and visibility is generally good on US 30 with the exception of the widened 
shoulder on the south side of US 30 at the intersection of Old Mill Town Road near the convenience 
store that is frequently utilized for large truck parking. In addition, ODOT currently owns all access 
rights along US 30 west of Westport. Therefore, any new access would be required to obtain a grant 
of access. 
Old Mill Town Road and Westport Ferry Road are the primary north‐south roadways through the 
northern portion of Westport (i.e., north of US 30). Neither roadway meets County standards, and 
both have narrow travel lanes, gravel shoulders, no sidewalks, and poor pavement conditions. Both 
roadways have an at‐grade crossing with the Burlington Northern rail line that runs parallel to US 
30. The crossings are two lanes with no sidewalks or bike lanes and are controlled by cross bucks 
and flashing lights (i.e., no crossing gates). Improvements to both roadways and railroad crossings 
may be required to safely accommodate any increase in traffic demand (particularly truck traffic). 
Potential improvements include roadway widening, railroad crossing enhancements, roadway 
signing and striping, horizontal curve realignments, sidewalks, and pavement reconditioning. 
Old Highway 30 is a minor road that accesses US 30 approximately one‐quarter mile west of 
Westport. It is a narrower road with minimal traffic and tees into US 30 on the south side of the 
highway. The new ferry access road being considered would be aligned with Old Highway 30 to 
create a four‐legged intersection.

Existing Traffic Volumes
Existing 2011 traffic volumes were determined at the three study intersections on US 30 in 
Westport: 
            US 30/Old Mill Town Road 
            US 30/Westport Ferry Road 
            US 30/Old Highway 30 

The volumes were determined by applying a seasonal adjustment factor and heavy vehicle 
percentage adjustments to existing count volumes. The result was the determination of 30th highest 
hour traffic volumes. 

Seasonal Adjustment Factor
To determine the 30th highest hour volumes (30HV) and design hour volumes (DHV) for this study, 
the ODOT Analysis Procedures Manual (APM) methodology was considered.15 Three methods are 
outlined in the APM for the calculation of the seasonal factor: 
            On‐Site Automatic Traffic Recorder (ATR) Method 
            ATR Characteristics Table Method 
            ATR Seasonal Trend Table Method 

When applicable, the On‐Site ATR Method is the best and most accurate method to use, followed by 
the ATR Characteristic Table Method, and then the ATR Trend Table Method. Due to a variety of 
factors, each of the methodologies was examined. Because none was found to be satisfactory, an 
ATR Hourly Method was determined to be the most appropriate for US 30 through Westport. The 
following sections describe the development of the seasonal factor for US 30 in Westport. This 
methodology and the resulting seasonal adjustment factor was reviewed and approved by ODOT.16

ATR Characteristics Table Method
The ATR Characteristics Table Method is the second preferred method for determining a seasonal 
adjustment factor. Using this method, the ATR Characteristics Table is consulted to determine if 
there are other ATRs across the state with similar characteristics as the section of US 30 through 
Westport. The Rainier ATR (05‐006) is categorized as having a Summer seasonal traffic trend, and US 
30 in Westport is also expected to have a Summer seasonal traffic trend. Therefore, conditions used 
to identify ATRs with similar characteristics includes Summer peak trending traffic, rural area type, 
two lane highway, and AADT within 10 percent of 7,600 vehicles. 18 Several ATRs had similar 
characteristics to the study roadway, but also had motor vehicle volumes that varied more than 10 
percent from the study roadways. Therefore, no other ATR locations were found to meet all of the 
applicable criteria for US 30 in Westport. 

ATR Seasonal Trend Table Method
The next method considered for determining the seasonal adjustment factor was the ATR Seasonal 
Trend Table Method. This method groups the ATRs throughout the state into various trend 
categories (e.g., Commuter, Coastal Destination, Summer, etc.) and provides trend information by 
category for the 1st and 15th of each month of the year (interpolation required for other dates).19 US 
30 through Westport is expected to have a Summer seasonal traffic trend, and given the project 
timeline, traffic counts were to be collected in the middle of February. Therefore, the 2010 Seasonal 
Trend Table indicates that a seasonal factor of approximately 1.41 would be needed. However, 1.41 
exceeds the 1.30 factor limit identified in the APM. Therefore, a count taken on an average day in 
the middle of February is not expected to provide sufficiently accurate turning movement 
information for estimating 30th highest hour conditions (even with a seasonal factor).

ATR Hourly Method
Because the other methods do not provide satisfactory seasonal adjustment factors, an additional 
method was considered. A close examination of hourly trend information at the Rainier ATR (05‐
006) from 2005 to 2009 indicated that the maximum hourly volumes in February typically occurs on 
the Friday p.m. peak hour prior to Presidents’ Day weekend and that these volumes compared 
significantly more favorably (i.e., between 5% and 16% difference) with the Rainier ATR’s 30th

Heavy Vehicle Percentage Adjustments
Because US 30 through Westport experiences a higher percentage of heavy vehicles during the 
summer months than in February, additional heavy vehicle percentage adjustments are needed in 
addition to the seasonal adjustment factor. Figure 2 shows the seasonal variation of daily truck 
volumes at the Rainier ATR (05‐006). Very long vehicles (i.e., trailer trucks) are roughly consistent 
throughout the year, but there is a significant peak of other long vehicles (i.e., buses, campers, and 
single unit trucks) during the summer months. Because operational analysis accounts for all heavy 
vehicles combined, the long vehicle volumes from the two categories were combined to determine 
that the summer has approximately 63% more heavy trucks than a higher volume day in February.20 
Therefore, a 1.63 factor was applied to the heavy vehicle percentages at the study intersections.

Existing Traffic Operations
Existing 30th highest hour traffic operating conditions were analyzed for the study intersections. 
Before the analysis results of the study intersections are presented, discussion is provided for two 
important analysis issues: intersection performance measures (definitions of typical measures) and 
required operating standards (as specified by ODOT, which is the agency with roadway jurisdiction). 

Intersection Performance Measures
Level of service (LOS) ratings and volume‐to‐capacity (v/c) ratios are two commonly used 
performance measures that provide a good picture of intersection operations. In addition, they are 
often incorporated into agency mobility standards. 
            Volume‐to‐capacity (v/c) ratio: A decimal representation (typically between 0.00 and 1.00) 
             of the proportion of capacity that is being used at a turn movement, approach leg, or 
             intersection. It is determined by dividing the peak hour traffic volume by the hourly capacity 
             of a given intersection or movement. A lower ratio indicates smooth operations and minimal 
             delays. As the ratio approaches 1.00, congestion increases and performance is reduced. If 
             the ratio is greater than 1.00, the turn movement, approach leg, or intersection is 
             oversaturated and usually results in excessive queues and long delays. 
            Level of service (LOS): A “report card” rating (A through F) based on the average delay 
             experienced by vehicles at the intersection.21 LOS A, B, and C indicate conditions where 
             traffic moves without significant delays over periods of peak hour travel demand. LOS D and 
             E are progressively worse operating conditions. LOS F represents conditions where average 
             vehicle delay has become excessive and demand has exceeded capacity. This condition is 
             typically evident in long queues and delays. 

Existing Operating Conditions
The existing 30th highest hour traffic operations at the study intersections were determined based 
on the 2000 Highway Capacity Manual methodology22 for unsignalized intersections. Table 6 lists 
the estimated average delay, level of service (LOS), and volume to capacity (v/c) ratio of each study 
intersection. All three study intersections have low v/c ratios and comfortably meet ODOT’s mobility 
standards.

Transit Service
Transit service is provided in Westport by the Sunset Empire Transit District (SETD) and the 
Columbia County Rider (CCR). The SETD bus route runs two times a day, Monday through Saturday 
between Astoria and Westport, with several stops in between. The CCR bus route runs two times a 
day, Monday through Friday between Westport and Kelso, Washington, with stops in Clatskanie and 
Rainier. The bus stop in Westport is located near the intersection of Old Mill Town Road/Highway 
30. This stop serves as a transfer point between the two bus services.

CHAPTER 4: FUTURE SCENARIOS
This chapter reviews the expected transportation conditions and improvement needs under three 
future year 2026 traffic scenarios: 
            Background Highway Growth Scenario 
            Local Development Scenario (includes highway growth) 
            New Collector Roadway Scenario (includes highway growth and local development) 

Each successive scenario builds on the previous scenario. Therefore, the assumptions and analysis 
results of the Background Highway Growth scenario are documented first, followed by the Local 
Development Scenario, and then the New Collector Roadway Scenario. A comparison of the three 
scenarios is then provided along with a planning recommendation. 

Background Highway Growth Scenario
The Highway Growth Scenario evaluates background growth on US 30 through the year 2026. It 
does not assume any significant development occurs in Westport. The analysis considers future 
traffic background volumes, intersection operations, turn lane warrants, and community livability.
