Connectivity is pursued as a transportation policy because it reduces out-of-direction travel,
provides multiple route options, disperses traffic, reduces arterial traffic loads, and facilitates
convenient walking, bicycling and transit access. Communities with high rates of walking and
bicycling tend to have good street connectivity in the form of grids or other connected
networks. By contrast, large blocks, cul-de-sacs and disconnected street networks tend to
discourage walking while increasing automobile traffic on arterial roadways.

Connectivity in new development in unincorporated Washington County is regulated by the
Community Development Code (CDC) and a series of Community Plans (the study area includes
portions of the Aloha-Reedville-Cooper Mountain Community Plan area and Sunset West
Community Plan area, in addition to areas regulated by the City of Beaverton).

The CDC promotes connectivity in new
development by regulating block lengths and
perimeters, limiting cul-de-sacs, and requiring
street or accessway (pedestrian/ bicycle)
connections to pre-existing street stubs. The
Community Plans build on these regulations by
mapping Local Street Connectivity Lands.

Ϯ. Undeveloped Public Rights-of-Way

Several public rights-of-way in the study area have never been developed as streets. These
missing connections, sometimes called “paper streets,” are in this case due to the natural
barriers imposed by stream corridors. Common practice in Washington County in the mid- to
late-ϮϬth century was to leave these riparian corridors mostly in tact. This had the effect of
protecting valuable natural resources and reducing infrastructure costs, but also created
connectivity gaps that remain to this day.

circumstances it may be more appropriate to provide a pedestrian/bicycle connection only; and

space. Recommendations for undeveloped public rights-of-way within the study area are listed

4. Redevelopment

Additional local street and pathway connections may be possible through redevelopment of
properties that are indicated in Metro’s Buildable Lands Inventory (BLI) as having capacity for
additional development. The BLI is based on an analysis of existing development density
compared to allowable density in the local zoning code, and in consideration of natural hazards
and community facilities that are expected to remain unchanged. For commercial properties
the BLI includes a market element, identifying those properties in which land values exceed
building values.

Please note: Private property owners have the ultimate authority on whether or not they
redevelop their properties, when they would do so, and how the future land uses and streets
would be arranged on their sites to meet applicable county or city development codes.

Water Quality and Detention Report
                           Water Quality and Detention Design Narrative


We have reviewed the 170th Ave. and Merlo Rd. corridors and conceptual profiles and have identified
the potential locations for water quality/detention sites. Four water quality/detention sites have been
identified to treat/detain the increase in impervious areas of the 170th Ave and Merlo Rd projects. The
calculations assume a five lane section with 6’ bike lane and 8’ sidewalk on both sides of the street for
170th Ave and Merlo Rd corridors. Detention modeling have not been completed for these sites to
determine the dimension of these facilities. The approximate sizes of these facilities have been based
on the modeling for 158th and Jenkins projects, applying the proportionate factor of drainage basin
impervious to determine the facility dimension for 170th Ave and Merlo Rd projects. The four facility
locations with approximate dimension are listed below:

    1.   East of 170th Ave and South of Sullivan Ln: 18’ wide x 250’ long.
    2.   East of 170th Ave at Vendla Park Ln: 30’ wide x 270’ long.
    3.   North of Merlo Rd and East of Merlo Dr: 18’ wide x 185’ long.
    4.   West of Merlo Rd and North of railroad track on PGE property: 15’ wide x 140’ long.

1st Location:

This water quality/detention pond will treat the impervious surface on 170th Ave corridor between SW
Merlo Rd and W Baseline Rd but is located in a wetland area and will need to be under the roadway or
the wetlands will need to be mitigated.

2nd Location:

This water quality/detention pond will treat the impervious surface on 170th Ave corridor between SW
Alexander St and SW Merlo Rd.

3rd Location:

This water quality/detention pond will treat the impervious surface on Merlo Rd corridor between SW
170th Ave and approximately 1200 feet north of Merlo Ct (crest point of Merlo Rd).

4th Location:

This water quality/detention pond will treat the impervious surface on Merlo Rd corridor between the
street crest point (see location 3) and SW Jenkins Rd.



The design has not utilized pervious sidewalk as an option to reduce the imperviousness for these
projects. If this method is selected, there will be some reduction in the dimension of the facilities
identified above. Also, the facility at location #4 could be combined with the 158th/Jenkins project to
create a regional facility that can serve both projects. The attached exhibits show the locations of the
facilities identified and discussed above.

Washington County has identified the 170th Avenue / Merlo Road corridor as an area of needed
improvementinordertobetterserveitsdiversityofusers.The170thAvenue/MerloRoadConceptual
DesignPlanprojectwilldevelopaconceptualdesignforthesetwoconnectingarterialroadways,based
on an analysis of existing conditions, opportunities, and constraints; a broader look at surrounding
neighborhoodcontext;anevaluationofbestpracticesandinnovativedesigns;andaninclusivepublic
involvementprocess.TheconceptualdesignwillprovideWashingtonCountyandcorridorstakeholders
withahigherlevelofcertaintyastohowthecorridorwilllookandfunctioninthefutureandwillbetter
preparetheCountyfordesigning,engineering,andconstructingimprovementsinthecorridor.

The existing conditions memorandum documented the existing physical, policy and regulatory
conditionsofthecorridor.Thismemorandumidentifiespotentialdesignsolutionalternativesforeach
corridor segment and in consideration of the identified existing conditions and project objectives.
Informationandgraphicscontainedhereinwillbeusedinthepublicengagementprocess–including
Project Advisory Committee meetings and community workshops – to elicit feedback and inform
decisionsonwhichalternativeswouldbebroadlysupportedbythecommunity.Thismemorandumis

MULTIMODALANDSTORMWATERDESIGNELEMENTS
As noted in the existing conditions memorandum, Washington County has key objectives for the
conceptual design plan that support the overarching goal to provide multimodal circulation; support
adjacentlanduseanddevelopment;andaddressanumberofenvironmentalconstraints.Thedesign
elementsoutlinedinthismemorandumseektoaddresseachofthespecificprojectobjectives.


BicycleandPedestrianFacilityTreatments
This section includes examples of pedestrianͲ and bicycleͲspecific facilities, as well as crossing
treatments.Potentialbicycleandpedestrianfacilitytreatmentswereselectedtoaddressthefollowing
projectobjectives:

Objective1:Createacorridorthatwillencourageandsupporttheuseofactivetransportationmodes
andreducerelianceontheautomobile.

Objective 2: For the purpose of bicycle and pedestrian mobility, examine the local street and trail
network to address safety and connectivity, including connections necessary to link adjacent
developmenttoanyalternativeparallelbicycleorpedestrianfacilities.

Objective 3: Explore the application of innovative bikeway treatments for 170th Avenue and Merlo
Road,potentiallyincludingatwoͲwaycycletrack,oneͲwaycycletracks,orbufferedbicyclelanes.

Objective4:Providesaferoutesandcrossingsforbicyclistsandpedestrianstoareadestinationssuchas
BeaverAcresElementarySchool,MerloStationHighSchool,TualatinHillsNaturePark,theMerlo/158th
AveMAXStation,andtheElmonica/170thAveMAXStation.

Objective5:Exploreprojectphasingoptionsthatcoulddeliverpedestrian/bicyclefacilitiesinadvanceof
plannedroadwaywidening.

TransitFacilityTreatments
Segments2and3oftheprojectareplannedtohavebustransitserviceinthefuture,andtherefore,the
concept design plan needs to consider transit stops and interactions with other modes along the
corridor.

Objective 6: Explore opportunities to accommodate bus stops, including improved pedestrian access,
along170thAvenueandMerloRoadinanticipationofaTriMetͲproposedbusline.


TransitStops
TriMet’sexistingbusstopspacingguidelinesindicate780feetindenseareasand1000feetinlowͲtoͲ
mediumdensityurbanareas.Toachievethisspacingalonganewroute,stopsshouldbelocatedfirstat
transfer locations, such as the existing MAX stations in the corridor. Next, stops should be placed at
existingdesignatedcrossings(suchassignalizedintersections).Finally,stopsshouldbeplacedattransit
trip generators, such as commercial destinations or dense residential areas, as close as possible to a
crosswalk.

Everytransitstopisrequiredtohaveabusstopsign,anditispreferredthateachhaveanAmericans
withDisabilityAct(ADA)landingpadandrearlandingpad.Otheramenitiesareofteninstalledaswell,
depending on anumberof factors,e.g., ridership. These amenities may include a shelter, benches, a
trashcan,serviceinformation,orlighting.Toallowforthepossibilityofshelters(2.5feetͲ4.5feetwide)
at high ridership locations, sufficient space would need to be allocated to provide the clearance
requiredbythebusstopguidelines:2feetbetweenshelterandcurb;12feetfrombuildings,fencesor
otherstructures.FurtherdetailsondesignofstopscanbefoundinTriMet’sBusStopsGuidelines.

North170th(Segment1)
Therearebicyclelanesonbothsidesof170thAvenuenorthofWhitleyWay,butunfortunatelybicycle
laneswerenotprovidedalongthesegmentbetweenBaselineRoadandWhitleyWay.Havingbicycle
facilities on each side of 170th Avenue to the south of Baseline Road would enable a fairly simple
transition across the Baseline Road/170th Avenue intersection, and would provide easy access to the
relatively dense new residential development on the west side of 170th Avenue. Designated as an
EnhancedMajorStreetBikeway,thesegmentshouldhavebufferedbicyclelanesorcycletracksinthe
future.

CONCEPTUALEVALUATIONOF170THAVENUE/MERLOROADINTERSECTION
Thestudycorridorconsistsoftwointersectingarterialroadways,eachofwhichintersectswithother
arterialsattheterminiofthestudycorridor.Thisprojectwillassesstheoptionsprimarilyforthe170th
Avenue/Merlo Road intersection. For intersections at the termini of the corridor, it will recommend
solutionstoprovidesmoothtransitions.Atahighlevel,eachoftheintersectiontypesdescribedherein
can be designed to provide safe multiͲmodal access; however, each type has advantages and
disadvantagesforapplicationinthisparticularlocation.Table8introducesfourgeneraloptionsforthe
intersection.

           1) maintainingtheexistingfourͲlegsignalizedintersection;
           2) converting the intersection to a threeͲleg intersection in its current location by
              disconnectingaccesstothewestleg;
           3) realigningtheintersectiontoathreeͲlegintersectionwiththroughmovementsfrom170th
              Avenue(southofMerloRoad)toMerloRoad;and
           4) constructingamultilaneroundabout.

CONCLUSIONANDNEXTSTEPS
The 170th Avenue / Merlo Road corridor presents a variety of options for Washington County to
consider.Thismemorandumprovidesexamplesofdesigntreatmentstoaddressbicycleandpedestrian
travelaswellasgreenstreetstreatments.Thealternativesandsegmentcrosssectionsshowhowthese
elementscouldbeappliedinthecorridor.Thenextstepsintheprojectaretorefinethedirectionof
theconceptualdesignforthecorridorasfollows:

     x     WashingtonCountystafftoreviewdesignoptionsandprovidefeedback.
     x     Technical/publicadvisorycommitteemeetingtoreviewoptionsandprovidefeedback.
     x     Community members to indicate preferences and provide other ideas and feedback at the
           communityworkshop.
     x     Projectteamtofurtherrefinedesignconceptforfavoredoption.
