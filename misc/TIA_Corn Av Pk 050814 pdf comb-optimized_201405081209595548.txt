Executive Summary

The Avery Park site is located on Bailey Road east of Bailey Springs Road and across from Hough High School
in Cornelius, North Carolina, and is currently vacant. A single-family development is proposed to include between
97 and 99 lots. A vicinity map and the conceptual site plan are included in Appendix A. A stub-out is proposed to
the north to connect to Bartlette Creek Drive, and the project is planned for build-out by 2017.

This study was conducted per the requirements of the North Carolina Department of Transportation (NCDOT),
and the recommendations of the study are:
          - SR 115 (Old Statesville Road)/Bailey Road – The southbound left turn lane should be restriped to
              increase turn lane storage to the maximum that allows NCDOT standard lanes and tapers within
              the existing pavement, and full width storage of 210 feet should be provided for the westbound
              right turn lane. Sketches are provided in Appendix E.
          - Bailey Road/Hough High School/site entrance – The painted median should be restriped to provide
              an exclusive turn lane of 200 feet into Avery Park. Separate left and right turn lanes should be
              provided on the exit from Avery Park, and right turn storage of 100 feet should be provided. A
              sketch of the left turn lane is provided in Appendix E.

Introduction

The Avery Park site is located on Bailey Road east of Bailey Springs Road and across from Hough High School
in Cornelius, North Carolina, and is currently vacant. A single-family development is proposed to include between
97 and 99 lots. A vicinity map and the conceptual site plan are included in Appendix A. A stub-out is proposed to
the north to connect to Bartlette Creek Drive, and the project is planned for build-out by 2017.

Capacity analyses were conducted to assess the existing, no build, and build-out volumes at the study
intersections identified by NCDOT:
           - SR 115 (Old Statesville Road)/Bailey Road
           - Bailey Road/Hough High School Driveway/site entrance

The study was conducted for morning and afternoon peak hours of adjacent street traffic. In this report, Bailey
Road is referred to as an east-west road, and SR 115, Hough High School Driveway, and site entrance are
referred to as north-south.

Existing Conditions

Existing conditions at the study intersections are shown in Figure 1. At the study intersections, all roads have one
through lane in each direction. There are existing left and right turn lanes into and out of the High School
driveway. Traffic exiting the school driveway is controlled by a stop sign. At the SR 115 intersection, there are
left turn lanes on SR 115, a right turn lane on Bailey Road westbound, and a left turn lane on Bailey Road
eastbound. A railroad runs parallel to SR 115 at its intersection with Bailey, and the signal at the intersection has
railroad pre-emption. The speed limit on Bailey Road in the study area is 35 miles per hour, and the speed limit
on SR 115 is 45 miles per hour. There are no CAT routes adjacent to the site. Therefore, only travel by personal
automobile will be considered in this study.

Background Conditions

Background, or no build, traffic is the traffic that would be at the study intersections in the future without the
proposed development. Background traffic is made up of existing traffic and any increase or decrease in volumes
which might occur from general growth trends in the surrounding area or from nearby specific developments. In
consultation with NCDOT, general traffic growth was estimated at two percent per year for three years and is
shown in Figure 3.

In addition, during neighborhood meetings for this project, three specific projects were mentioned:
           - Davidson East – This project is located on NC 73 well east of the subject site. The traffic study for
                Davidson East was not available while this study was being conducted. Because the information
                was not available, and because the project is well away from this site, any traffic from Davidson
                East was assumed to be included in the two percent per year growth.
           - Bailey Springs and Bailey’s Glen – These developments are located west of the subject site. The
                traffic studies for these developments were not available, but a traffic study for Hough High School
                was available and included the traffic from these developments as background traffic. Because
                the single family portion of Bailey’s Glen appears to be about one third complete, and the single
                family portion of Bailey Springs appears to be about 70 percent complete, the trip generation
                estimated in the High School study was adjusted as shown in Appendix C. From other information
                in the High School study, it appeared that the trip distribution for Bailey’s Glen and Bailey Springs
                was 50 percent to/from the west, 35 percent to/from the northeast, and 15 percent to/from the
                southeast. That distribution was applied to the trips shown in Appendix C, and the corresponding
                background trips are shown in Figure 3.

The 2017 no build peak hour volumes are the sum of the volumes discussed above and are also shown in Figure
3.

There are no planned roadway projects at the study intersections.

Capacity Analyses

To this point, this report has regarded the quantity of traffic flow, but the purpose of the report is to examine the
quality of traffic flow. The methodology used in this study for assessing the quality of traffic flow is the
methodology described in the 2000 Highway Capacity Manual (HCM), Transportation Research Board. In
general, the HCM expresses quality of flow in terms of Level of Service (LOS). The types of transportation
facilities which will be examined in this study are the signalized and unsignalized intersections. The criteria for
signalized intersection LOS is shown in Table 2, and the criteria for unsignalized intersection LOS is shown in
Table 3. The variable used is control delay. This is the delay attributed to traffic control measures and includes
deceleration delay, queue move-up time, stopped delay, and final acceleration delay. Although at a signalized
intersection LOS D is usually considered the lowest acceptable overall LOS, it is not unusual for a side street at
an unsignalized intersection to experience LOS E or F during the peak hour. It is suggested, therefore, that the
results of the unsignalized intersection analyses be used as guidance rather than as absolute measures of delay.

NCDOT requires that mitigation be considered when comparing no build to build conditions if at least one of these
conditions exists: the total average delay on an individual approach increases by 25 percent or more while
maintaining the same LOS, the LOS degrades by at least one level, or the LOS is F. The instances in which
these conditions exist at the study intersections are highlighted.

The study intersections were analyzed for morning and afternoon peak hours with existing, no-build, and build
traffic volumes. Percentages of heavy vehicles were taken from existing counts. Because peak hour factors for
future conditions are required to be 0.90 by NCDOT, the peak hour factors for existing conditions at the SR 115
signal were also considered to be 0.90 so that no-build conditions would not look better than existing conditions in
the afternoon peak hour. However, the peak hour factors at the school drive were maintained at existing values
because there is no delay there except in school peak times. Therefore, delay for the site entrance will be
overstated except for the peak 15 minutes of school traffic. Grades were estimated from field visits, and lane
widths were measured on aerial photographs. Green times were optimized for existing conditions, and clearance
intervals were estimated. Capacity analysis printouts are included in Appendix D.

Turn Lane Storage

While the LOS at an intersection can describe the basic operation of traffic, other factors influence that operation.
For instance, if turn queues extend past their storage, they can interrupt traffic flow. Therefore, an estimate of the
turn storage needs is needed, and the required lane lengths were estimated for the no-build and build volumes
using the 95 percentile queues from SIMTRAFFIC as shown in the printouts in Appendix D. A comparison of
these turn storage needs and the available storage lengths are shown in Table 6 and indicates that storage
needs for several movements will exceed existing storage with no build traffic, but the addition of site traffic
makes little difference in storage needed. These movements are of note:
           - WB Right from Bailey to SR 115 – Although this lane is wider than normal and can accommodate a
                 vehicle past the 140 feet of turn lane marked, NCDOT requests that this turn lane be widened to
                 provide a full lane of 210 feet with required tapers. A sketch is provided in Appendix E.
           - NB Left from SR 115 to Bailey – This left turn lane drops immediately into a two-way left turn lane,
                 providing extra storage in peak times.
           - EB Left from Bailey to SR 115 – The future storage needs are not excessive, and no vehicles are
                 added to this movement by Avery Park.
           -     SB Left from SR 115 to Bailey – This is the movement for which LOS will change from D to E in the
                 afternoon peak hour with the addition of site traffic. Therefore, it is recommended that the existing
                 striping on the approach to this left turn bay be revised to increase the left turn storage from 150
                 feet to 190 feet. A sketch is provided in Appendix E and indicates that the storage could be further
                 extended within existing pavement and requests that the storage length be extend to the maximum
                 that allows NCDOT standard lanes and tapers within the existing pavement.
           -     EB left from Bailey into Avery Creek – The existing center striped median will be restriped to
                 provide this lane. NCDOT requests that the storage be maximized. Therefore, storage of 200 feet
                 is recommended. A sketch of the left turn lane is provided in Appendix E.

Potential for New Traffic on Bartlette Creek Drive

Connectivity is desired in Cornelius as evidenced by the requirement for connections between subdivisions.
However, during neighborhood meetings for Avery Park, concerns were raised about additional traffic which may
be added to Bartlette Creek Drive, the Bailey Springs street to which the Avery Park streets will connect. The
movement for which this street will be most attractive is the high school exit movement during school dismissal.
During the morning, the delay which is expected for left turns out of Avery Park, while reasonable for exiting a
residential development in a peak hour, will not be attractive to entering high school traffic in the morning.

To estimate the potential for high school student cut-through in the afternoon, a count was taken of left turns out
of the school which then turned right into Bailey Springs during the peak school dismissal. That count indicated
that seventeen percent of the lefts out of the high school driveway then turned right into Bailey Springs. The
future volume of the left turn is 84, and seventeen percent of 84 is 14. Therefore, this route may be attractive to
about 14 vehicles per hour during school dismissal.
