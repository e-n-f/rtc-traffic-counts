     This memorandum documents transportation analysis of the Wallace Road (Highway 221)/Glen
     Creek intersection performed for the City of Salem as part of the 2008 Streets and Bridges
     Project. The purpose of the analysis is to determine the future intersection improvements.
     The analysis includes the existing 2010 traffic conditions, a description of improvement
     alternatives, an analysis of 2015 Year of Opening conditions for each alternative, and an analysis
     of 2032 Future conditions and turn lane storage needs.
     2010 Existing Conditions
     An analysis of existing conditions was performed for the study intersections under 2010 30th
     Highest Hourly Volumes (30th HV). In this section, the estimation assumptions for the 2010 30th
     HV are explained followed by the 2010 existing operating conditions.

     2010 Existing 30th Highest Hour Volumes
     The existing traffic volumes were collected at four study intersections to assure impacts to/from
     the Wallace Road adjacent signalized and unsignalized intersections were included in the
     evaluation of the Wallace Rd/Glen Creek Rd intersection.1 Counts were taken on April 7, 2010
     during the a.m. (7:00 a.m. to 9:00 a.m.) and p.m. (4:00 p.m. to 6:00 p.m.) peak periods. The a.m.
     and p.m. peak hour traffic volumes used for the analysis are shown in Figure 1, and detailed peak
     period traffic counts are included in the Appendix. Traffic volumes along Wallace Road were
     also seasonally adjusted to reflect 30th Highest Hour Volumes consistent with ODOT procedures.
     The nearest ODOT Automatic Traffic Recorder (ATR) 24-014 was used, as approved by City
     and ODOT staff.2 This resulted in a seasonal adjustment factor of 1.01.

2010 Existing 30th Highest Hour Operating Conditions
Existing intersection operations for 2010 30th HV conditions were analyzed at the Wallace
Road/Glen Creek Road intersection and compared with ODOT intersection operating standards.
The statewide highway classification for Highway 221 (Regional Highway) corresponds to a
mobility standard requiring operation during the peak hour at or below a volume to capacity (v/c)
ratio of 0.85 for speeds <= 35 MPH.3
The traffic operations were determined based on the 2000 Highway Capacity Manual
methodology4 for signalized intersections. The level of service (LOS) and volume to capacity
(v/c) ratio for both the a.m. and p.m. peak hours are shown in Table 1. As shown, the Wallace
Road/Glen Creek Road intersection currently does not meet ODOT operating standards.

Description of Improvement Alternatives
Four different intersection improvement alternatives were considered (in addition to the No-
Build alternative) and evaluated for the Wallace Road/Glen Creek Road intersection as shown in
Figure 2:
          Alternative 1 increases the footprint of the intersection by adding a second left turn lane,
           and dedicated right turn lane to the northbound approach (south leg). The eastbound
           approach (west leg) includes a dedicated left turn lane, and the through lane becomes a
           through-right lane along with the existing right turn lane. The current left-through-right
           lane on the westbound approach (east leg) is split into two lanes (left-through and
           through-right) for a total of three approach lanes. Due to the shared through-left turn
           lanes, the signal is run as split phase in the east-west directions.
          Alternative 1b expands on the first alternative and modifies the eastbound approach so
           there are no shared lanes. The final lane configuration on the eastbound approach is a
           single left turn lane, through lane, and dual right turn lanes.
          Alternative 2 looked at making the intersection completely 8-phase by separating the
           shared left-through travel lanes (no split phasing). The eastbound left-through was
           modified to a dedicated left turn lane and through lane. The westbound approach was
           modified to have two left turn lanes and a through-right lane. The northbound approach
           was modified similar to Alternative 1, with two left turn lanes and a dedicated right turn
           lane.
          Alternative 3 is the same as Alternative 2, but includes dual eastbound right turn lanes.

Future Operations Analysis
Future conditions analysis for the year 2015 and 2032 was performed to determine the preferred
improvement alternative for the Wallace Road/Glen Creek Road intersection. The 2015 scenario
corresponds to the estimated construction completion year for the selected improvements, and
the 2032 scenario consistent with state and federal requirements for a 20-year evaluation. This
section discusses the 2015 Year of Opening and 2032 Future operating conditions, as well as the
2032 Queuing Analysis.
Traffic growth was evaluated based on information collected from City of Salem historical
counts, the Mid-Willamette Valley Council of Government (MVCOG) SKATS model, and
ODOT ATR stations. This provided a wide range of growth alternatives for Wallace Road and

Through discussion with the City of Salem and ODOT it was determined that an acceptable
alternative should optimize operational performance between the a.m. and p.m. peak periods.6 As
shown, Alternatives 1b and 3 have the lowest a.m. peak hour v/c ratios, and have similarly low
p.m. peak hour v/c ratios compared to the other two alternatives. While Alternative 2 has the
lowest p.m. peak hour v/c ratio, it has a high a.m. peak hour v/c ratio, and is therefore not as
desirable as Alternative 1b or 3.

2032 Queuing Analysis
To compare the storage length needs of alternatives at the Wallace Road/Glen Creek Road
intersection, queuing analysis was performed for each turning movement in each alternative.
The analysis was performed using SimTraffic™, which provides a system wide assessment of
network performance and includes the estimation of the 95th percentile queues. The 95th
percentile queue is the queue length at a given intersection movement that has only a 5% chance
of being exceeded during the peak traffic hour. It is standard engineering practice to use the 95th
percentile queue length for sizing storage lanes for turn pockets.
Figure 3 shows the estimated queue lengths for each movement of the Wallace Road/Glen Creek
intersection for each of the alternatives. Highlighted movements have a v/c ratio greater than 1.0.
When the v/c is greater than 1.0 this means the allotted green time cannot serve the estimated
traffic levels during the peak hour and can lead to increased queuing.
As shown, the key difference between the alternatives is that eastbound right-turn queuing in
Alternative 1 and 2 exceeds 300 feet during the a.m. and p.m. peak hours. These alternatives
have one dedicated eastbound right-turn lane compared to the two right-turn lanes shown in

Summary
Analysis indicates that the critical constraint of the Wallace Road/Glen Creek Road intersection
is the amount of through capacity on the northbound and southbound approaches. Future
forecasts on Wallace Road show the need for three northbound and three southbound through
lanes. Until a long-term solution to this issue is constructed, the operational level of the
intersection will continue to exceed ODOT standards in the future.
The future intersection operations and queuing analyses documented in this memorandum
indicate that Wallace Road/Glen Creek Road intersection operations can be improved through
turning movement additions and improvements at the intersection approaches. Of the four
alternatives selected for analysis, Alternative 1b and Alternative 3 showed similar operational
and queuing performance. They shared the following characteristics:
      Two northbound left turn lanes
      A dedicated northbound right turn lane
      Two eastbound right turn lanes
      A dedicated eastbound left turn lane
Alternative 1b and 3 differed in how they improved the westbound approach. Alternative 3 used
two left turn lanes instead of the left and left-through used by Alternative 1b. This led to
differences in signal timing (split phasing vs. 8-phase) and the slight difference in operational
performance and queuing.
As mentioned, these improvements will not be enough to provide operations within ODOT
standards in 2032; however they will provide improved operational levels as compared to the no-
build scenario.
