Executive Summary




The US 97/La Pine Corridor Plan addresses multi-modal and capacity needs for the downtown La Pine
corridor between 1st Street and 6th Street. The plan presents a near-term and longer-term vision of
improvements that can enhance the safety, comfort, convenience and efficiency of pedestrian, bicycle,
vehicular and freight travel in and through the downtown. This Plan will serve as a pre-cursor to the
City's Transportation System Plan (TSP).

The project goals, key findings and recommendations associated with this plan are summarized below.


Project Goals
          Identify safety improvements to the pedestrian crossings occurring near 1st Street

          Develop a strategy to accommodate La Pine growth and economic development, particularly
          within the industrial parks

          Improve east-west pedestrian crossings throughout the corridor

          Reduce the barrier created by the highway for east-west auto travel

          Reduce speed differentials and improve the rural to urban transition at the northern and
          southern edges of the downtown core

          Develop detailed project scoping reports to allow identified projects to be eligible for funding

          Develop a plan that balances the Statewide Highway and Freight Route roles with the
          downtown


Key Study Findings
          Frequent pedestrian and bicycle crossings occur at the US 97/First Street intersection
          throughout the course of the day. There are no delineated pathways or crosswalks at the
          intersection and field observations reveal frequent "near misses" between the pedestrians and
          oncoming traffic.            This situation is further compounded by high vehicular speeds from
          southbound traffic entering the city from the rural segment of US 97.

          Many of the existing intersections along US 97 intersect the highway at a skewed angle, which
          make pedestrian crossings and turning onto and off of the highway difficult.

          There are continuous sidewalks on the eastside of US 97 but not on the west. The presence of
          multiple driveways along the corridor presents conflicts between pedestrians and turning
          motorists.

          There are no bicycle lanes along US 97 so cyclists must share the road or walk their bicycles
          along the sidewalk.

          The 1st Street ­ Reed Road, Huntington Road, Finley Butte, and 6th Street intersections with US
          97 meet minimum volume thresholds for the installation of a traffic signal today.

           Although the highway has five-lanes in areas of the downtown core, the curb-side lanes are
          primarily for used for passing or decelerating maneuvers. The wide striped shoulder toward the
          north end of the City serves as a deceleration area. This wide cross-section makes pedestrian
          and bicycle crossings of the highway difficult and provides a level of discomfort for those
          walking or cycling alongside the highway.

          The number of recorded crashes in the US 97 corridor is relatively low; several of the crashes
          that occurred happened under adverse roadway and weather conditions. A single fatality was
          recorded near the 6th Street intersection.

          La Pine is planning for significant industrial and employment growth on the east side of the city
          during the next twenty years. This growth will further exacerbate the multimodal connectivity
          and capacity needs near the US 97/First Street intersection. This growth will also require
          multimodal connectivity on city streets that parallel US 97 to provide residents with non-
          highway options to access their homes and jobs.


Preferred Corridor Recommendations
The preferred corridor alternative was identified through public input and the project advisory team
comprised of stakeholders and jurisdiction staff. A description of the improvements included as part of
the preferred alternative are summarized below.

          The US 97 cross-section should only include one travel lane in each direction and a center turn
          lane throughout the downtown core, as illustrated in Exhibit 1-1.

          The US 97/1st Street intersection should be signalized. As part of signalization, the First Street
          and Reed Road approaches should be realigned, pedestrian refuge islands should be
          constructed on the south approach, intersection approach treatments should be added to allow
          drivers to transition from a high-speed rural environment to the downtown, and additional turn
          lanes should be constructed. An illustration of the concept is provided in Figure 1-1.

          The Finley Butte/Morson Street/US 97 intersection should be realigned and signalized. The
          four-legged intersection would include separate eastbound and westbound left-turn lanes, as
          illustrated in Figure 1-2.

          The intersection of 4th Street/US 97 will be a focal point of multi-modal connectivity in the
          downtown. The intersection may ultimately include high visibility pedestrian treatments, such
          as a rapid rectangular flashing beacon. The preferred concept eliminates the northbound left-
          turn maneuver to accommodate a pedestrian refuge island in a center median, as illustrated in

          Interim treatments at the US 97/1st Street intersection range from low-cost maintenance

Existing Conditions




This chapter presents information on existing traffic volumes, reported collisions, roadway cross-
section characteristics, signage, and bicycle and pedestrian infrastructure. This chapter also
summarizes intersection performance, signal warrants (as a proxy for improvement needs), and
roadway segment capacity.


Data Collection
Data for the existing conditions assessment was collected from a variety of sources. The US 97 corridor
through downtown La Pine was visited and inventoried in September 2010; during the site visit
information was collected on posted speed, pedestrian signage, roadway cross-section, auxiliary lanes,
access locations, sidewalk and bicycle lane presence, and public intersection characteristics.

Traffic counts used for the operations analysis were collected by ODOT in October 2009; the traffic
count data obtained includes the impact of the new McDonald's restaurant on the corner of US 97/Reed
Road while school was in session. All of the counts along the corridor were videotaped and compiled
between 4:00 a.m. and 10:00 p.m. to fully capture the volume profile and characteristics throughout the
day.

Roadway safety information was also obtained from ODOT for the most recent five-year period on
record (January 2005 through December 2009). The safety data included a summary of crashes at the
study intersections and segment crashes along the US 97 corridor.


Existing Transportation Facilities
This section provides a summary of the transportation infrastructure within La Pine, detailing the
existing roadway cross-section, posted speeds, presence of bicycle lanes and sidewalks, and access. This
section is intended to describe the presence and connectivity of the City's existing multi -modal
facilities.


ROADWAY CROSS-SECTION

The cross-section of US 97 varies throughout the study area. Heading southbound on US 97 from the
Wickiup Junction US 97 has a 55 mph posted speed and functions as a two-lane rural highway facility
with no development or commercial access on either side of the road. This rural segment transitions to
a four-lane segment providing passing opportunities in both the northbound and southbound
directions. Following the transition back to a two-lane section there is an approximately 3/4 mile

segment prior to reaching 1st Street. Within this short segment, there is advance and regulatory signage
for the speed transition from 55 mph to 35 mph. The highway begins to widen immediately north of 1 st
Street with a striped median transition, providing a full northbound left-turn lane as the road
transitions to a three-lane cross-section.

South of 4th Street, US 97 transitions into a five-lane cross-section that extends south to 6th Street. At 6th
Street the curbside lane in the southbound direction becomes a right-turn only drop-lane as the
highway transitions back into a three-lane section through the segment containing the City offices, then
into a two-lane rural segment south with a speed transition back to 55 miles per hour.

The northbound travel direction provides a similar cross-section. However, north of 4th Street the
northbound width remains wide enough to accommodate an additional travel lane, but the area is
striped as a wide shoulder (17 feet) with parking not allowed. This area is commonly used as a
deceleration lane for local business access. Figure 2-1 illustrates the existing cross-section for US 97.


PEDESTRIAN AND BICYCLE FACILITIES

The sidewalks along the east side of US 97 are continuous due to their construction as part of a joint
Deschutes County and ODOT streetscape project prior to the City's incorporation. On the west side of
US 97 there is a partial sidewalk system connecting 1st Street to 6th Street. Sections of sidewalk have
poor surface conditions and contain a number of undefined access conflicts with private businesses.
Near Morson Street angled head-in parking conflicts with the sidewalks, and the limited parcel depth of
certain businesses require internal circulation to occur within the sidewalk area.

By Oregon law unmarked extensions of public intersections are legal crosswalks and require driver
yielding. However, due to the skew and offset of various intersections many pedestrians cross the
highway at non-intersection locations to take advantage of gaps in traffic volumes on the highway.
There is a marked crosswalk at the 4th Street ­ William Foss Road intersection on the south side of the
intersection. This crossing is located within a school zone and contains advance signage, overhead
pedestrian signage with a flashing yellow beacon, and post-mounted signage, as shown in Photo 4-2.

There are no striped bicycle lanes along the US 97 corridor within La Pine. Bicyclists were observed
traveling on the sidewalks and on the edge of the curbside travel lane.

ACCESS AND DOWNTOWN PARKING

A detailed inventory of the existing access locations, driveway widths, and an inventory of the parking
supply was provided within the La Pine Special Transportation Area (STA) Plan1 that was reviewed as
part of the project literature review (the project literature review is included in Attachment 1). The STA
Plan identified a total of 42 approaches (including nine public street approaches) onto US 97. These
driveways were shown to vary in width from 10 feet to 50 feet. Field observations identified that
segments of the highway contain undefined approaches that allow access throughout the property
frontage. Newer developments in La Pine contain frontage improvements that include defined access
points and detached sidewalks.

While there is no on-street parking allowed along US 97 through the City, there are a number of surface
parking lots serving adjacent businesses. Due to the shallow depth of lots near the US 97/Morson Street
intersection, angled parking is allowed directly in front of the buildings.


ROADWAY RIGHT-OF-WAY

US 97 right-of-way maps were obtained to understand potential constraints with the interim and long-
term solutions identification. The existing right of way for US 97 is 300 feet wide to the north of 1st
Street, with the highway alignment located in the eastern portion of the right of way. South of 1st Street
the highway right of way is reduced to 100 feet through town. US 97 Right of Way maps are included in
Attachment 4.

The crashes were then plotted and reviewed by area along the corridor. Historical crash records are

US 97/Morson Street Intersection

The intersection of US 97 with Morson Street is skewed at a nearly 45 degree approach angle. Morson is
a two-lane roadway that provides a direct connection between US 97 and 1st Street. The posted speed
on Morson Street is 25 miles per hour. Adjacent to the intersection with US 97 is an access into the
West View motel. Due to the close spacing between the private access and US 97, drivers are required
to stop approximately 25 feet from in advance of the intersection to avoid blocking the driveway.

There was only a single reported crash at the intersection. A rear-end crash occurred during snowy
conditions and resulted in an injury. No crash patterns were identified at the intersection. While
adequate sight lines are available, due to the intersection skew angle and stop-bar location the difficulty
in viewing oncoming traffic is increased. Further, turning movements do not contain consistent speeds
due to the angles.

ODOT SPIS/SIPS REVIEW

The Safety Priority Index System (SPIS) is a method developed by ODOT for identifying potential safety
problems on state highways. SPIS is a tool used to identify accident history in 0.10 mile or variable
length segments on state highways. SPIS scores are developed based upon crash frequency, severity,
and rate. A prioritized list is created for each region (the top 10 percent of statewide SPIS sites) and is
provided annually for analysis and possible corrective action. Available information on the ODOT
TransGIS site did not identify any of the study intersections as SPIS locations within the 2006 to 2008
period.


Traffic Volume Data
Traffic volume data for the US 97 corridor was reviewed from a variety of sources, including current
and historical turning movement counts, tube counts, and automatic traffic recorder (ATR) data from
nearby permanent count stations. Traffic volumes in the La Pine area fluctuate considerably throughout
the year due to seasonal tourist activity, winter road conditions, school impacts, and economic
conditions. For this study, Design Hour Volumes (DHV) were developed to replicate 30 th highest hour
conditions. The 30th highest hour is a typical design volume that avoids the bias of peak holiday or
weekend traffic while still enabling an analysis reflective of summertime conditions. Turning
movements counts are included in Attachment 6.

SIGNAL WARRANT 3: PEAK-HOUR VEHICULAR VOLUME

Peak hour delays are used to identify locations where undue delay is experienced by minor street
vehicles entering or crossing the major roadway. This signal warrant is only applicable in unusual
cases, such as with sites that experience heavy peaking characteristics, such as office complexes,
industrial facilities, or similar developments.

Two separate conditions can be satisfied to meet Peak Hour Signal Warrants. Under the first condition,
three conditions must all be met: 1) the total stopped time delay experienced by the traffic on one
minor-street approach (one direction only) controlled by a STOP sign equals or exceeds five vehicle
hours, and 2) The volume on the same minor-street approach (one direction only) equals or exceeds
150 vehicles per hour for two moving lanes of traffic, and 3) the total entering volume serviced during
the hour equals or exceeds 800 vehicles per hour for an intersection with four approaches.

The level of service worksheets show that the first condition is not met based on peak hour delays of
less than one hour on the critical approach of each of the intersections. The second condition allows the
use of Figure 4C-4 within the MUTCD. Based on review of the graph, peak hour signal warrants are met
at all of the intersections except US 97/6th Street.

Conclusions
The US 97 corridor serves as a critical north-south freight route and inter-regional connection. Within
downtown La Pine businesses are reliant on the highway for access. Despite the commercial zoning on
inter-related uses, parcels along the corridor contain limited inter-connectivity, wide and undefined
driveways, and are missing sidewalk segments along the west side of the highway. More recently
developed parcels include detached sidewalks and off-street parking.

Volumes profiles show that the highway experiences its peak demands during the lunch period, with
relatively constant volumes throughout the remainder of the afternoon and into the commute period.
Truck traffic comprises approximately fifteen to twenty percent of the overall traffic volumes
throughout the day.

Highway crossings for pedestrians and motorists are difficult with observed delays beyond those
predicted in the analysis models due to the speed transition, crossing width, highway skew, and offset
approaches. Despite the higher congestion there are relatively few reported crashes within the La Pine
core, though review of intersection videotapes identified a number of near-misses at the US 97/1st
Street intersection. Safety concerns at this intersection are increased due to the number of pedestrian
crossings and lack of driver yielding. Overall, crashes within La Pine are more likely to occur during
snow and ice than other parts of the County, and are more likely to result in injury than non-injury.
Nearly 80 percent of all reported crashes are turning and angle crashes, likely due to the stop-control
on the highway approaches.

Based on the STA Plan, costs associated with full implementation of a streetscape design could total
upwards of $6.1 million. To fund and implement the streetscape improvements it is recommended that
the City of La Pine adopt the recommended cross-section and require half-street frontage

should be considered along parcels where modifications would be discontinuous, or to minimize
construction impacts to the traveling public.

A project phasing strategy could include development of the three-lane cross-section with signing,
striping, and temporary curbing, with the changes being made permanent and expanded to include
illumination and roadside swales and full-width sidewalks with redevelopment. The creation of a
continuous and accessible sidewalk system along the west side of US 97 should be part of any initial
roadway improvement plans.

Illumination could be provided in phases along the US 97 corridor, with illumination at intersections
and within school zones forming an initial project phase, illumination at the US 97 rural to urban
transition a second phase, and illumination throughout the corridor as part of a final phase. Property
development or redevelopment should require illumination be provided along the frontage. To ensure
a consistent design the City, in collaboration with ODOT, should identify luminaire design standards
(including identification of decorative details), spacing, and allow bonding in lieu of construction where
the luminaires cannot be situated in their ultimate locations.

The recommended central pedestrian refuge islands and medians can be individually prioritized and
constructed as stand-alone projects. Prioritization should be given to school zone crossings and
locations experiencing high pedestrian activity.


Conclusions
A continuous pedestrian and bicyclist system will require modifications to the existing US 97 cross-
section. The system should be designed to accommodate snow storage within landscaped swale areas.
The width of these swales may vary along the corridor to allow continuous 8-foot sidewalks and 6-foot
bicycle lanes.

With the corridor cross-section modifications highway crossings can be provided at regular intervals
throughout the City core, with crossings at 1st Street, at the Ray's Grocery Store, at 3rd Street, 4th Street ­
William Foss, Huntington Road, the realigned Finley Butte ­ Morson Street intersection, and at 6th
Street. Crossings at the Ray's and Huntington Road could accommodate a two-stage crossing with a
median refuge.

Streetscape plans for the US 97 corridor should include illumination between 1 st Street and 6th Street,
with additional lighting along the rural to urban transition. Illumination should be carefully sited near
pedestrian crossings, at intersections, and near pedestrian generators.

anticipated system performance with the existing system still in-place, and with the potential "build"
options. This chapter also summarizes the opportunities and constraints associated with each of the
options from a multi-modal, cost, phasing, environmental, and project lifespan perspective.


Traffic Volume Forecast
A variety of methods can be used to develop future traffic volumes along the corridor, ranging from an
extrapolation of historical trends to detailed modeling analyses based on projected population and
employment growth. A number of potential forecasting methodologies were reviewed determine the
most appropriate for use along the study corridor. The following describes the derivation of the
methodology for the study.


HISTORICAL GROWTH TRENDS ON US 97

Various traffic counts are historically available to identify traffic growth at key intersections along the
US 97 corridor through downtown La Pine. These traffic counts provide a snapshot of conditions on a
particular day, and can vary substantially by day of week and even seasonally. To obtain a larger
sampling of traffic volumes along the corridor ODOT conducts scheduled traffic counts at critical
locations throughout the State system on a rotating three-year basis. Volumes in the years that counts
are not conducted are compiled based on nearby permanent traffic count stations, and this compiled
data is summarized within the Traffic Volume Tables. The closest locations to the La Pine core are
immediately north of 1st Street (milepost 167.49) and immediately north of OR 31. Annual Average
Daily Traffic (AADT) volumes from the location near 1st Street were reviewed between 1996 and 2009
as summarized in Exhibit 4-1.

Review of the AADT data identified annual growth rates along the US 97 corridor north of 1st Street

should be noted that the regional growth does not account for increased trips that remain within the La
Pine core area, only regional through trips destined to and from Bend and Sunriver.


DESCHUTES COUNTY TRAVEL DEMAND MODEL

For medium-sized and larger communities, travel demand models most often form the basis for
estimating future traffic volumes. These models use projected household and employment growth to
estimate traffic volumes on collector and arterial facilities throughout a city, MPO or County. A regional
model for Deschutes County is currently being developed by ODOT's Transportation Planning and
Analysis Unit (TPAU) for use in the County's Transportation System Plan Update. Currently there isn't a
sufficient level of detail in this model for the La Pine area to adequately assess alternatives for the US
97 corridor so alternative forecasting methods were utilized.

CUMULATIVE ANALYSIS METHOD

A cumulative analysis is also based on land use growth but can be a simpler approach than travel
demand models to forecasting traffic volumes. ODOT staff developed a cumulative model for the
Wickiup Junction Railroad Overcrossing Project. This forecasting model was prepared in 2006, prior to
the County's TSP and the City's Comprehensive Plan efforts, and considered growth through 2032. The
study identified a range in projected annual growth in traffic volumes within the downtown La Pine
core between 1.0 and 2.7 percent (compounding growth). At the time of the project economic forecast
and population data suggested that the majority of the future growth in La Pine would be associated
with residential uses, with the highest growth projected along Finley Butte Road toward the east and
little growth expected to occur from the industrial lands.

More recently, the City's current Comprehensive Plan and investments have focused the City's
development efforts and goals toward balancing employment needs within La Pine. The "shovel-ready"
designation of the industrial lands provides the potential for significant employment opportunities
within the planning period. These are expected to help retain vehicular trips within the City, reducing
the City's reliance on US 97 for commuting to other areas in the County and increasing the localized
traffic within City core.

While the overall range of growth between 1.0 and 2.7 percent is generally consistent with the
historical growth trends along US 97, the shift in future growth from residential to employment will
alter the orientation of trips along the US 97 corridor and increase the turning movements and US 97
crossing demand within the downtown core.


RANGE FORECASTING

Although the City's Comprehensive Plan and zoning show significant growth potential within La Pine,
the timeframe for build-out of the City's Urban Growth Boundary will likely occur well beyond 2032.
According to Deschutes County Ordinance 2009-006, the coordinated population forecast for La Pine
grows from 1,697 in 2010 to 2,352 in the year 2025. The County extrapolated this population, based on
a 2.2 percent annual growth rate, to 2,566 people in the UGB in 2029 (2,735 if extrapolated to the
corridor study horizon year of 2032).

To ensure flexibility and maximize transportation investments, the US 97 corridor plan uses a range
forecast to estimate future volumes. This will ensure that the plan can adapt to changing growth
conditions, reflect increased employment opportunities within the City's industrial parks, and provide
reasonable assurances on the lifespan of transportation infrastructure investments.

FUNDED IMPROVEMENTS

Currently, there are no projects within the City of La Pine downtown core that can be considered
funded for the purposes of the No Build analyses. The 2010-2013 Statewide Transportation
Improvement Program (STIP) lists improvements at the US 97 intersection with 1st Street - Reed Road.
However, the funding for this project is allocated for design and right-of-way acquisition only

connections to Burgess and Rosland Road, and will substantially alter the operations of US 97 within

In addition, improvements to the corridor may result in "latent demand" on the corridor. This latent

Improvement Safety

Quantitative safety methods are not available to thoroughly analyze the benefits of these types of
interim improvements. However, it is reasonable to expect that these improvements would help drivers
better judge acceptable gaps in the US 97 corridor and could thereby improve driver comfort and safety
at the intersection, both in reported crashes and "near-misses."

Pedestrian comfort and safety would be improved through the installation of a pedestrian refuge. This
treatment would improve visibility of pedestrians, reduce the crossing distance and exposure, provide
a defined crossing location, and help to manage vehicle speeds along US 97. In addition, as considered
in all the options, the extension of sidewalks on the west side of US 97 and improved connection to
sidewalks along the north side of 1st Street would improve the quality of service provided to
pedestrians.

Improvement Cost and Phasing Strategies

The overall option is estimated to cost approximately $1.1 million to construct. However, the
improvements could be phased-in over time, providing flexibility to respond to changing traffic
conditions and funding availability. Likely phasing options could include:
          Widening US 97 to add a southbound left-turn lane (estimated cost: $185,000, does not include
          the median treatment)

          Improvements to the 1st Street and Reed Road approaches, including realigning the
          intersection approaches, providing channelized right-turn lanes on the westbound and
          eastbound approaches, providing pedestrian signing treatments, intersection illumination,
          cross-section changes on US 97 to the south, new sidewalks, and a pedestrian median refuge on
          US 97 (estimated cost: $400,000)

          Changes to the transition areas on US 97, including rumble strips, recessed raised pavement
          markers, transverse striping, speed radar sign, and a southbound left-turn lane taper median
          (estimated cost: up to $55,000)

          Transition area illumination (estimated cost: approximately $80,000)

          Monument sign (separate City-funded project).

The projects listed above would likely need to be constructed in the order listed, with exception of the
monument sign and illumination, both of which could be implemented independent of any other
improvements. The southbound left-turn lane is needed today (primarily for safety) but significant
