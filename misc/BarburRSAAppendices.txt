Barbur Concept Plan
 • Long-term vision for a more
    “walkable, vibrant Barbur”
 • Six-mile corridor from
    Portland's Central City to the
    Tigard city limit
 • Recommendations focusing on
   two major approaches:
    – Fix the safety problems that exist today
    – Look for opportunities to catalyze development
      through investments in transit, parks, trails,
      stormwater, pedestrian and bicycle access and other
      infrastructure

High Crash Corridor Safety Plan
• 2000-2009 identified Barbur
  as a street with “a high
  incidence of traffic crashes,
  injuries and fatalities”
• Safety plan identified:
   • Key findings
   • Corridor-wide
     improvements
   • Enforcement and education
     activities




                                                                      16
                                                                       7/17/2015




High Crash Corridor Safety Plan (cont.)
  • Key findings:
       • Driveway crashes are about 50% higher than the citywide
         average
       • Driveway crashes generally ‘turning’ or ‘angle’ crashes
       • Incidence of crashes caused by drivers disregarding traffic
         signals is about 50% higher than the citywide average
       • Approximately 50% of the corridor lacks sidewalks
       • Bike network is incomplete on many structures along
         Barbur

Barbur/OR-99W Corridor
Safety & Access to Transit
• 2016-2018 STIP project
• Design and construct modifications to improve
  safety, access to transit, and active transportation
• Potential improvements:
   • Sidewalk infill
   • Enhanced pedestrian crossing at
     Barbur Blvd and Lane/Naito Pkwy
   • Enhance existing bus stops
   • Retaining walls and drainage
     improvements
   • Bus activated extended green time
     at up to 16 signals




Southwest Corridor Plan
 • Package of transit, roadway, bicycle and pedestrian
   solutions
 • Targeted at reducing congestion, improving circulation
   and improving quality of life in the corridor
 • Supports local land use visions adopted by each
   community
 • Involvement of community members, business leaders,
   transit providers, the state and local governments

High Crash Corridor Safety Plan
• 2000-2009 identified Barbur
  as a street with “a high
  incidence of traffic crashes,
  injuries and fatalities”
• Safety plan identified:
   • Key findings
   • Corridor-wide
     improvements
   • Enforcement and education
     activities




High Crash Corridor Safety Plan (cont.)
• Key findings:
   • Driveway crashes are about 50% higher than the citywide
     average
   • Driveway crashes generally ‘turning’ or ‘angle’ crashes
   • Incidence of crashes caused by drivers disregarding traffic
     signals is about 50% higher than the citywide average
   • Approximately 50% of the corridor lacks sidewalks
   • Bike network is incomplete on many structures along
     Barbur
   • Generally, bus stops are located near pedestrian crossing
     improvements
   • Four outbound bus stops are greater than a ¼ mile from
     any crossing improvement

Barbur/OR-99W Corridor
Safety & Access to Transit
• 2016-2018 STIP project
• Design and construct modifications to improve
  safety, access to transit, and active transportation
• Potential improvements:
   • Sidewalk infill
   • Enhanced pedestrian crossing at
     Barbur Blvd and Lane/Naito Pkwy
   • Enhance existing bus stops
   • Retaining walls and drainage
     improvements
   • Bus activated extended green time
     at up to 16 signals




Barbur/OR-99W Corridor
Safety & Access to Transit (cont.)
 • Anticipated benefits:
     • Improve access, safety, and travel time on TriMet lines
       12, 64, and 94
     • Encourage transit use
     • Link employees to office, retail, and service sector
       jobs in the OR-99W corridor to several TriMet bus lines
     • Serve a diverse base, including low income
       communities
     • Facilitate safe crossings at non-signalized intersections
       and at mid-block locations
     • Increase capacity for other roadway users, particularly
       freight, by increasing HOV travel
