SUBJECT:         Alder Street Transportation Analysis                                                        P13160-000



This memorandum documents the transportation analysis performed to determine the preferred cross-section
                                                    th
for the segment of Alder Street extending from 5 Avenue to Palouse Street in Walla Walla, Washington. The
purpose of this evaluation is to provide a recommendation for which alternative should be advanced to design
and construction. This analysis is based on the following two alternative cross-sections identified in coordination
with City staff (detailed layouts are provided in the appendix):

        •   Three-lane cross-section including one lane in each direction, a two-way left-turn lane, angle parking
            along the north side, and parallel parking along the south side.
        •   Two-lane cross-section including one lane in each direction and angle parking along both sides.

This memorandum documents the project background, study area, existing roadway conditions, analysis of
future alternatives and a recommendation as to the preferred alternative.

Collision Analysis 
Collision analysis was performed at the study intersections to identify baseline safety conditions. Five years of 
collision data (2007‐2012) were obtained from the Washington Department of Transportation and are 
summarized in Table 3. Except for the 2nd Avenue/Alder Street intersection, angle collisions are the most 
frequent type at the study intersections. Because signalized intersections are typically characterized by more 
rear‐end collisions and less angle collisions, it is likely that poor signal head visibility is contributing to the 
collisions at these locations. There was also one collision involving a pedestrian and one involving a bicyclist, 
both at the intersection of Alder Street and 2nd Avenue. 

Typically, a collision rate greater than or equal to 1.0 collision per million entering vehicles (MEV) indicates a 
higher than average collision rate. As shown, none of the study intersections have a collision rate in excess of 
the 1.0 collision per MEV threshold. Low traffic volumes and speeds along this segment of Alder Street 
contribute to keeping the collision rates less than 1.0.

Queuing Analysis 
Traffic simulation was performed in SimTrafficTM to evaluate queuing impacts associated with reducing the 
number of travel lanes on Alder Street. Table 5 on the following page summarizes the eastbound (EB) and 
westbound (WB) 95th percentile queues for each future scenario. The 95th percentile queue is the queue length 
for a given intersection movement that has only a 5% chance of being exceeded during the peak traffic hour, 
and it is standard engineering practice to use the 95th percentile queue when evaluating storage needs.  

As expected, queue lengths for the two‐lane and three‐lane configurations would be longer than those expected 
if the existing four‐lane configuration was maintained. During the p.m. peak hour, there are significantly longer 
queues associated with the two‐lane configuration at 2nd Avenue and 1st Avenue. The increased length of these 
queues is the result of left‐turn vehicles blocking through traffic (there is no turn lane in the two‐lane 
configuration) while waiting for an acceptable gap to complete the maneuver. The westbound 95th percentile 
queue at 2nd Avenue during the p.m. peak hour is estimated to be 335 feet, which could extend into the 
upstream intersection with 1st Avenue (approximately 300 feet away). Congestion from the 2nd Avenue 
intersection contributes to the increased westbound queues shown at 1st Avenue.

Safety 
In addition to operational impacts, the proposed cross‐sections are expected to affect safety for pedestrians, 
bicyclists, and motor vehicles along the corridor. When considering motor vehicles, the ability to remove left‐
turn vehicles from a shared lane provides substantial safety benefits. This applies to each study intersection as 
well as the numerous other intersections and driveways along the corridor. Even though the majority of vehicles 
travel below the posted speed of 25 mph, both alternative cross‐sections would eliminate the option to pass 
slower vehicles in the adjacent lane and are expected to reduce the number of vehicle travelling above the 
speed limit.  

Furthermore, the Highway Safety Manual includes a crash modification factor (CMF) for converting a four‐lane 
roadway to three lanes, and estimates that this conversion will result in a 29% decrease in the number of 
collisions along the corridor. The Highway Safety Manual does not include a CMF for converting a four‐lane 
roadway to two‐lanes; however it is not expected that this conversion would result in a significant safety 
improvement (an increase in collisions would not be expected with a four‐lane to two‐lane conversion). 

Because bike lanes are not included in either of the proposed cross‐sections, cyclists will be required to share 
the travel lane with vehicles. Therefore, neither alternative presents an obvious benefit for cyclists. In terms of 
pedestrian safety, both alternatives would reduce the distance pedestrians are exposed to traffic with the 
installation of curb extensions (offsetting parking) and the reduced travel lane width on Alder Street. 

On‐Street Parking 
In addition to improving safety and operations, reducing the number of travel lanes along Alder Street allows for 
the creation of additional on‐street parking. Currently, there is parallel parking along both sides of the road, 
totaling approximately 96 parking stalls between 5th Avenue and Spokane Street. The three‐lane alternative 
would include changing the parallel parking along the north side of Alder Street to diagonal parking, which 
would create approximately 47 additional parking stalls. The two‐lane section would also change the parallel

Truck Loading and Unloading 
In the current four‐lane configuration, trucks currently park in the right travel lane adjacent to on‐street parking 
to load and unload goods for adjacent businesses. The second travel lane allows vehicles to pass the stopped 
truck with little delay. Under the two‐lane and three‐lane alternatives, the second travel lane will not be 
available for truck loading. Truck loading will need to be coordinated with downtown businesses to determine if 
loading can take place on side streets or if a striped loading zone would need to be included as part of the 
improvement alternative. Adding loading zones would require the removal of some on‐street parking stalls.   

Recommendations 
The operational analysis indicates that both alternatives would have sufficient capacity to support future traffic 
volumes along Alder Street. Based on the queuing analysis, the two‐lane alternative would result in long queues 
at the Alder Street/2nd Avenue intersection, and that left‐turn lanes are needed at this intersection to alleviate 
congestion in the downtown. Because left‐turn lanes are not needed at the remaining intersections, a hybrid of 
the two alternative cross‐sections is recommended. Therefore, Alder Street should be converted to two lanes 
from 5th Avenue to Palouse Street, with widening at the Alder Street/2nd Avenue intersection to accommodate 
left‐turn lanes (see appendix for conceptual layout of the 2/3 lane hybrid alternative).  

The inclusion of left‐turn lanes at 2nd Avenue will increase the capacity of this intersection and reduce queuing 
and congestion along Alder Street in downtown Walla Walla. Table 7 on the next page summarizes intersection 
operations and queuing for the recommended 2/3 lane hybrid alternative. As shown, intersection operations are 
the same as those provided in previous tables for the corresponding lane configuration, and all intersections 
operate within City standards. In addition, the left‐turn lanes accommodate future traffic volume growth 
without queuing or congestion concerns.

TRAFFIC LEVELS OF SERVICE
Analysis of traffic volumes is useful in understanding the general nature of traffic in an area, but by itself
indicates neither the ability of the street network to carry additional traffic nor the quality of service
afforded by the street facilities. For this, the concept of level of service has been developed to subjectively
describe traffic performance. Level of service can be measured at intersections and along key roadway
segments.

Level of service categories are similar to report card ratings for traffic performance. Intersections are
typically the controlling bottlenecks of traffic flow and the ability of a roadway system to carry traffic
efficiently is generally diminished in their vicinities. Levels of Service A, B and C indicate conditions
where traffic moves without significant delays over periods of peak travel demand. Level of service D and
E are progressively worse peak hour operating conditions and F conditions represent where demand
exceeds the capacity of an intersection. Most urban communities set level of service D as the minimum
acceptable level of service for peak hour operation and plan for level of service C or better for all other
times of the day. The Highway Capacity Manual provides level of service calculation methodology for
both intersections and arterials. 1 The following two sections provide interpretations of the analysis
approaches.




   1
       2000 Highway Capacity Manual, Transportation Research Board, Washington D.C., 2000, Chapters 16 and 17.
SIGNALIZED INTERSECTIONS

For signalized intersections, level of service is evaluated based upon average vehicle delay experienced by
vehicles entering an intersection. Control delay (or signal delay) includes initial deceleration delay, queue
move-up time, stopped delay, and final acceleration delay. In previous versions of this chapter of the HCM
(1994 and earlier), delay included only stopped delay. As delay increases, the level of service decreases.
Calculations for signalized and unsignalized intersections are different due to the variation in traffic
control. The 2000 Highway Capacity Manual provides the basis for these calculations.

      Service   (secs.)                     Description
    ─────────────────────────────────────────────
        A       <10.00        Free Flow/Insignificant Delays: No approach phase is fully utilized by traffic and no vehicle waits
                              longer than one red indication. Most vehicles do not stop at all. Progression is extremely favorable and
                              most vehicles arrive during the green phase.

         B          10.1-20.0          Stable Operation/Minimal Delays: An occasional approach phase is fully utilized. Many drivers begin
                                       to feel somewhat restricted within platoons of vehicles. This level generally occurs with good progression,
                                       short cycle lengths, or both.

         C          20.1-35.0          Stable Operation/Acceptable Delays: Major approach phases fully utilized. Most drivers feel somewhat
                                       restricted. Higher delays may result from fair progression, longer cycle lengths, or both. Individual cycle
                                       failures may begin to appear at this level, and the number of vehicles stopping is significant.

         D          35.1-55.0          Approaching Unstable/Tolerable Delays: The influence of congestion becomes more noticeable.
                                       Drivers may have to wait through more than one red signal indication. Longer delays may result from
                                       some combination of unfavorable progression, long cycle lengths, or high v/c ratios. The proportion of
                                       vehicles not stopping declines, and individual cycle failures are noticeable.

         E          55.1-80.0          Unstable Operation/Significant Delays: Volumes at or near capacity. Vehicles may wait though several
                                       signal cycles. Long queues form upstream from intersection. These high delay values generally indicate
                                       poor progression, long cycle lengths, and high v/c ratios. Individual cycle failures are a frequent
                                       occurrence.

         F          >80.0              Forced Flow/Excessive Delays: Represents jammed conditions. Queues may block upstream
                                       intersections. This level occurs when arrival flow rates exceed intersection capacity, and is considered to
                                       be unacceptable to most drivers. Poor progression, long cycle lengths, and v/c ratios approaching 1.0 may
                                       contribute to these high delay levels.
