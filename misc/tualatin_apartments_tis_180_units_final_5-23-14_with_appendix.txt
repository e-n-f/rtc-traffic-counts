CHAPTER 1: INTRODUCTION AND SUMMARY
This study evaluates the transportation impacts for the proposed Tualatin Apartments project located at 
17895 SW Pacific Highway (OR 99W) in Tualatin, Oregon. The proposed 180 multi‐family apartment units 
are located on 8.6 developable acres just south of the Tualatin River.  
The purpose of this transportation impact study (TIS) is to evaluate transportation impacts the proposed 
development would have on the nearby transportation network. The impact analysis is focused on 
intersections identified as being in the study area which are also show in Figure 1. These study 
intersections were identified by the Oregon Department of Transportation (ODOT)1 and were 
coordinated with the City of Tualatin.

Proposed Development
Located in Tualatin, Oregon, the project site is approximately 8.6 acres in size and is currently in the 
process of being rezoned to High‐Density Residential (RH). Currently, the site is mostly vacant with a

project sponsor has proposed to construct 180 multi‐family apartment units with a new roadway 
connection to SW Pacific Highway (OR 99W).

Intersection Performance Measures
Level of service (LOS) ratings and volume‐to‐capacity (v/c) ratios are two commonly used performance 
measures that provide a good picture of intersection operations. In addition, they are often 
incorporated into agency mobility standards. 
        Level of service (LOS): A “report card” rating (A through F) based on the average delay 
         experienced by vehicles at the intersection.3 LOS A, B, and C indicate conditions where traffic 
         moves without significant delays over periods of peak hour travel demand. LOS D and E are 
         progressively worse operating conditions. LOS F represents conditions where average vehicle

            evident in long queues and delays. 
           Volume‐to‐capacity (v/c) ratio: A decimal representation (typically between 0.00 and 1.00) of 
            the proportion of capacity that is being used at a turn movement, approach leg, or intersection. 
            It is determined by dividing the peak hour traffic volume by the hourly capacity of a given 
            intersection or movement. A lower ratio indicates smooth operations and minimal delays. As the 
            ratio approaches 1.00, congestion increases and performance is reduced. If the ratio is greater 
            than 1.00, the turn movement, approach leg, or intersection is oversaturated and usually results 
            in excessive queues and long delays.

database. The data identified 46 collisions within the study area during the five‐year period. A 
breakdown of the collisions by intersection and severity is provided in Table 6. 
As shown, the greatest number of collisions occurred at the SW Pacific Highway (OR 99W)/SW 124th 
Avenue intersection with 34 total crashes, with no fatalities and one injury A. All intersections were 
below the general threshold of 1.0 collisions per million entering vehicles (MEV) used to indicate 
potential safety concerns.

The Safety Priority Index System (SPIS) is a method ODOT uses to perform screening on the state 
highway network to identify potential safety problems and prioritize improvement. The SPIS score is 
based on crash rate, frequency and severity over the prior three calendar years. SW Pacific Highway (OR 
99W) from milepost 12.57 to 12.75, which includes the intersection of SW Pacific Highway (OR 99W)/SW 
124th Avenue, falls within the top 5% of SPIS sites in Oregon5. The major factor in the SPIS score was a 
2010 rear end collision in the westbound direction at this intersection that resulted in an injury A. 

Public Transit Service
The study area is serviced by TriMet bus routes 93 and 94 west of the project access. These routes 
provide service to the study vicinity via a bus stop just west and one 350 east of the SW Pacific Highway 
(OR 99W)/SW 124th Avenue intersection. Route 93 serves the Tigard Transit Center, SW Pacific 
Highway/SW Durham Road, SW Tualatin‐Sherwood Road/SW Pacific Highway, SW Railroad Street/SW 
Washington Street, and others with approximately 30‐minute headways. Route 94 operates weekdays 
with 15‐minute headways from 5:30 a.m. to 9:00 a.m. and one‐hour headway until 6:30 p.m. Route 94 
serves the same areas noted for Route 93 with the addition of SW Main Street/SW Commercial Street, 
Barbur Transit Center, SW 6th Avenue/SW Yamhill Street, and SW 6th Avenue/W Burnside Street. 

Field Observations
The project area was observed during the a.m. and p.m. peak conditions. At the median crossover 
locations, u‐turning vehicles were observed but rarely more than one vehicle was queued at a time. 
Minimal delay was observed for the u‐turn vehicles as they did not wait long to enter oncoming traffic.  
The right‐turn lane on the eastbound approach of SW Pacific Highway (OR 99W) did not exceed the 
existing storage, however the through queue extended past the storage entrance. All other queue 
lengths at this intersection did not exceed existing storage and were able to completely clear during 
green phases. Few pedestrians were observed, many of who were walking to the transit stop west of the 
SW Pacific Highway/SW 124th Avenue intersection.

CHAPTER 4: PROJECT IMPACTS
This chapter reviews the impacts that the proposed 180 multi‐family unit apartment development would 
have on the study area transportation system. The focus of the impact analysis is on the following study 
intersections: 
           SW Pacific Highway (OR99W)/SW 124th Avenue 
           SW Pacific Highway (OR99W)/Median Crossover @ MP 12.77 
           SW Pacific Highway (OR99W)/Median Crossover @ MP 12.33 
           SW Pacific Highway (OR99W)/Proposed Primary Access 
 
This analysis includes trip generation, trip distribution, and future year traffic volumes and operating 
conditions. This chapter also includes a site plan review.  

Proposed Development
The proposed development is a 180 multi‐family unit apartment complex located on 8.6 development 
acres northwest of SW Pacific Highway (OR99W) and south of the Tualatin River in Tualatin, Oregon. A 
proposed driveway approximately 700 feet east of the SW Pacific Highway (OR 99W)/SW 124th Ave 
intersection on SW Pacific Highway (OR 99W) will provide access to the project site. This access will be 
restricted to right‐in/right‐out movements due to the existing median. 

Trip Generation
Trip generation is the method used to estimate the number of vehicles added to site driveways and the 
adjacent roadway network by a development during a specified period (i.e., such as the p.m. peak hour). 
Trip generation estimates are performed using trip rates surveyed at similar land uses, as provided by 
the Institute of Transportation Engineers (ITE).7 Table 8 lists the a.m. and p.m. peak hour vehicle trip 
generation estimates for the proposed 180 unit Tualatin Apartments. The project site is within 0.25 mile 
of a bus transit corridor, therefore a 5% reduction will be applied to the projected trips as described by 
the ITE Trip Generation Handbook.8 It is estimated that the project site would generate 1,140 average 
daily trips including 87 (17 in, 70 out) in a.m. peak hour and 106 (69 in, 37 out) in the p.m. peak hour. 
These trips were added to the roadway network for the future operations analysis to determine whether 
the site would impact the study intersections.

Site Plan Review
The site plan provided by the project sponsor was evaluated to identify potential concerns related to 
access, parking, and pedestrian facilities. A copy of the site plan is included in the appendix. 

Access and Driveway Width
A new proposed access on SW Pacific Highway (OR 99W) will provide connection to the proposed 
Tualatin Apartments. The driveway width would be 32 feet as required by the City10. The sight triangle at 
the driveway should be clear of objects (large signs, landscaping, etc.) that could potentially limit vehicle 
sight distance. All proposed site driveways should meet American Association of State Highway and 
Transportation Officials (AASHTO) intersection sight distance requirements11 as measured from 14.5 feet 
back from the edge of pavement. Prior to occupancy, intersection sight distance at the proposed 
driveway along SW Pacific Highway (OR 99W) will need to be verified, documented, and stamped by a 
registered professional Civil or Traffic Engineer licensed in the State of Oregon.  

Pedestrian Facilities
Frontage improvements along SW Pacific Highway (OR 99W) should be constructed in conjunction with 
this development.  Internal sidewalks provide convenient access to sidewalks along SW Pacific Highway 
(OR 99W).

TRAFFIC LEVELS OF SERVICE
Analysis of traffic volumes is useful in understanding the general nature of traffic in an area, but by itself
indicates neither the ability of the street network to carry additional traffic nor the quality of service
afforded by the street facilities. For this, the concept of level of service has been developed to subjectively
describe traffic performance. Level of service can be measured at intersections and along key roadway
segments.

Level of service categories are similar to report card ratings for traffic performance. Intersections are
typically the controlling bottlenecks of traffic flow and the ability of a roadway system to carry traffic
efficiently is generally diminished in their vicinities. Levels of Service A, B and C indicate conditions
where traffic moves without significant delays over periods of peak travel demand. Level of service D and
E are progressively worse peak hour operating conditions and F conditions represent where demand
exceeds the capacity of an intersection. Most urban communities set level of service D as the minimum
acceptable level of service for peak hour operation and plan for level of service C or better for all other
times of the day. The Highway Capacity Manual provides level of service calculation methodology for
both intersections and arterials. 1 The following two sections provide interpretations of the analysis
approaches.




   1
       2000 Highway Capacity Manual, Transportation Research Board, Washington D.C., 2000, Chapters 16 and 17.
UNSIGNALIZED INTERSECTIONS (Two-Way Stop Controlled)

Unsignalized intersection level of service is reported for the major street and minor street (generally, left
turn movements). The method assesses available and critical gaps in the traffic stream which make it
possible for side street traffic to enter the main street flow. The 2000 Highway Capacity Manual describes
the detailed methodology. It is not unusual for an intersection to experience level of service E or F
conditions for the minor street left turn movement. It should be understood that, often, a poor level of
service is experienced by only a few vehicles and the intersection as a whole operates acceptably.

Unsignalized intersection levels of service are described in the following table.

SIGNALIZED INTERSECTIONS

For signalized intersections, level of service is evaluated based upon average vehicle delay experienced by
vehicles entering an intersection. Control delay (or signal delay) includes initial deceleration delay, queue
move-up time, stopped delay, and final acceleration delay. In previous versions of this chapter of the HCM
(1994 and earlier), delay included only stopped delay. As delay increases, the level of service decreases.
Calculations for signalized and unsignalized intersections are different due to the variation in traffic
control. The 2000 Highway Capacity Manual provides the basis for these calculations.


      Level of  Delay
      Service   (secs.)                     Description
    ─────────────────────────────────────────────
        A       <10.00        Free Flow/Insignificant Delays: No approach phase is fully utilized by traffic and no vehicle waits
                              longer than one red indication. Most vehicles do not stop at all. Progression is extremely favorable and
                              most vehicles arrive during the green phase.

         B          10.1-20.0          Stable Operation/Minimal Delays: An occasional approach phase is fully utilized. Many drivers begin
                                       to feel somewhat restricted within platoons of vehicles. This level generally occurs with good progression,
                                       short cycle lengths, or both.

         C          20.1-35.0          Stable Operation/Acceptable Delays: Major approach phases fully utilized. Most drivers feel somewhat
                                       restricted. Higher delays may result from fair progression, longer cycle lengths, or both. Individual cycle
                                       failures may begin to appear at this level, and the number of vehicles stopping is significant.

         D          35.1-55.0          Approaching Unstable/Tolerable Delays: The influence of congestion becomes more noticeable.
                                       Drivers may have to wait through more than one red signal indication. Longer delays may result from
                                       some combination of unfavorable progression, long cycle lengths, or high v/c ratios. The proportion of
                                       vehicles not stopping declines, and individual cycle failures are noticeable.

         F          >80.0              Forced Flow/Excessive Delays: Represents jammed conditions. Queues may block upstream
                                       intersections. This level occurs when arrival flow rates exceed intersection capacity, and is considered to
                                       be unacceptable to most drivers. Poor progression, long cycle lengths, and v/c ratios approaching 1.0 may
                                       contribute to these high delay levels.
