1. INTRODUCTION

The purpose of this study is to evaluate the potential transportation impacts of the Castilian Redevelopment
Project, and identify improvements needed to mitigate deficiencies caused by the project.

PROJECT LOCATION AND DESCRIPTION

The Castilian Redevelopment Project will replace the vacant Castilian North and Castilian South student housing, a
500 unit complex for incoming freshman and transfer students being demolished due to seismic deficiencies, with
housing for up to 275 graduate students. The proposed project is located north of Russell Boulevard, between
State Route (SR) 113 and Wake Forest Drive - West 8th Street, on the UC Davis campus. The project location is
shown on Figure 1.

ANALYSIS SCENARIOS

The following scenarios were analyzed as part of this study:

         Existing Conditions

         Existing Plus Project Conditions

         Cumulative No Project Conditions

         Cumulative Plus Project Conditions

STUDY FACILITIES

Vehicle level of service was analyzed at the following study intersections (shown on Figure 1):

    1.   Russell Boulevard/State Route (SR) 113 Southbound (SB) Ramps
    2.   Russell Boulevard/State Route (SR) 113 Northbound (NB) Ramps
    3.   Russell Boulevard/Sycamore Lane
    4.   Russell Boulevard/Anderson Road ­ La Rue Road

to 9:00 AM) and PM (4:00 PM to 6:00 PM) peak periods when school was in session. The counts show that the AM
and PM vehicle peak hours occur from 8:00 AM to 9:00 AM and 5:00 PM to 6:00 PM, respectively.

Trip Generation Counts

Traffic volumes data was collected at the driveways of the Colleges at La Rue student housing complex at UC
Davis to develop trip generation rates for the proposed project. The Colleges at La Rue has three driveways
serving its designated parking spaces. Residents also park along Orchard Park Drive adjacent to the housing
complex. Video cameras recorded vehicles entering and exiting the driveways and on-street parking spaces on
Orchard Park Drive for a 24-hour period on May 15, 2012. Manual counts (recorded by a person) were conducted
during the AM (7:00 AM to 9:00 AM) and PM (4:00 PM to 6:00 PM) peak periods as well, to verify and calibrate the
video data. The camera recording the on-street parking spaces did not capture the all of the traffic entering and
exiting the spaces and was adjusted based on the manual count data.

TRAFFIC FORECASTS

Cumulative intersection turning movement forecasts were developed for the study locations using an updated
version of the Davis Travel Demand Model. The assumed cumulative land uses for UC Davis were consistent with
those assumed for the UC Davis 2003 Long Range Development Plan Final Environmental Impact Report (2003
LRDP EIR). The horizon year for the 2003 LRDP EIR is the 2015/16 academic year; however, it is unlikely that full
buildout of the 2003 LRDP EIR land uses will occur by the 2015/16 academic year. The model included the most
recent land use assumptions for the City of Davis based on the City of Davis General Plan. The difference method,
which adds the difference between the base and future year model runs to existing counts, was used to develop
the cumulative turning movement forecasts.

METHODOLOGY

Transportation engineers and planners commonly use the term level of service (LOS) to measure and describe the
operational status of the local roadway network. An intersection or roadway segment's level of service can range
from LOS A (indicating free-flow traffic conditions with little or no delay), to LOS F (representing oversaturated
conditions where traffic flows exceed design capacity, resulting in long queues and delays).

The study intersections were analyzed using the methodology presented in the Transportation Research Board's
Highway Capacity Manual (HCM) 2000. The 2010 Highway Capacity Manual includes minor updates to the HCM
2000 methodologies for signalized intersections; however, these updates do not affect the analysis results for
intersections in this study. Therefore, at signalized intersections, the HCM 2000 methodology was used.

Signalized Intersection Analysis

The HCM 2000 methodology determines the level of service (LOS) at signalized intersections by comparing the
average control delay (i.e. delay resulting from initial deceleration, queue move-up time, time actually stopped,
and final acceleration) per vehicle at the intersection to the thresholds shown in Table 1.

SIGNIFICANCE CRITERIA AND IMPACTS

Based on the UC Davis 2003 LRDP EIR, the following significance criteria was used to identify operational
deficiencies, if the proposed project would:

         Cause the LOS to change from an acceptable level to an unacceptable level. The following LOS thresholds
         apply to the study intersections:

             o    LOS D is the minimum acceptable LOS for UC Davis

             o    LOS C is the minimum acceptable LOS for SR 113 and its associated interchanges

         Add 10 or more vehicles to the volume of a study intersection that is currently operating at an
         unacceptable level

For this analysis, LOS C was used as the standard for the Russell Boulevard/SR 113 SB Ramps and Russell
Boulevard/SR 113 NB Ramps intersections (i.e. LOS A, B, and C are considered acceptable operations, while LOS D,
E, and F are considered unacceptable operations), and LOS D was used as the standard for the Russell
Boulevard/Sycamore Lane and Russell Boulevard/Anderson Road intersections (i.e. LOS A, B, C, and D are
considered acceptable operations, while LOS E and F are considered unacceptable operations).

INTERSECTION OPERATIONS

which utilizes HCM 2000 methodology. SimTraffic results are a product of a simulation that is designed to
represent "real-life" drivers to the best extent possible; therefore, each simulation run represents a unique set of
data. The results are based on an average of 10 runs.

BICYCLE AND PEDESTRIAN FACILITIES

Class I shared-use paths exist on the south side of Russell Boulevard through the entire study area, on the north
side of Russell Boulevard between Orchard Circle Park and Sycamore Lane, and on the west side of La Rue Road
south of Russell Boulevard. Class II bicycle lanes exist on Sycamore Lane and Anderson Road north of Russell
Boulevard.

Sidewalks exist on the north side of Russell Boulevard through entire study area with the exception of the section
between Orchard Circle Park and Sycamore Lane, which has a shared-use path. Sidewalks also exist on both sides
of Sycamore Lane and Anderson Road-La Rue Road.

The Russell Boulevard/SR 113 SB Ramps and Russell Boulevard/SR 113 NB Ramps intersections have crosswalks on
the north and south legs of the intersections (eastbound and westbound). Crosswalks are also located on all
vehicle approaches of the Russell Boulevard/Sycamore Lane and Russell Boulevard/Anderson Road intersections.

3. PROJECT CONDITIONS

TRIP GENERATION

Vehicle trip generation estimates were developed for the proposed Castilian Redevelopment Project based on 275
graduate student residents. The Colleges at La Rue is another student housing complex on the UC Davis campus
with similar characteristics to the proposed project. Vehicle trip generation counts were conducted at the
Colleges at La Rue to develop trip generation rates for the Castilian project.

The Colleges at La Rue has three driveways that serve its designated parking spaces. Residents also park along
Orchard Park Drive adjacent to the housing complex. Video cameras recorded vehicles entering and exiting the
driveways and on-street parking spaces on Orchard Park Drive for a 24-hour period. Manual counts (recorded by
a person) were conducted during the AM and PM peak periods to verify and calibrate the video data. Table 4
shows the trip generation rates developed based on the count data.

                                            TRIP GENERATION RATES PER RESIDENT

                   housing.

The trip rates shown in Table 4 are reflective of the fact that UC Davis has a high percentage of travelers who use
alternative modes of travel (e.g. walking, bicycling, transit, etc.) Based on surveys conducted in the fall of 2011,
the following percentages of students/faculty/staff living in the City of Davis or on the UC Davis campus use
alternatives modes of travel:

         Bicycle ­ 70%

         Transit ­ 19%

         Auto Driver ­ 11%

Traffic Forecasts

Traffic volume forecasts for the cumulative no project scenario were prepared using the Davis Travel Demand

generate traffic. To provide an accurate estimate of cumulative no project conditions forecasts, the land use
values associated with the existing Castilian buildings were removed before the model was run to represent the
vacant buildings.
